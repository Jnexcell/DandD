# World Building & the Multiverse

How to build the campaign setting your adventures live in — gods, maps, settlements, languages, factions, magic level, the sub-genres of fantasy, and the tiers of play — and then the structure of the planes of existence (the Great Wheel) that high-level characters eventually walk. This is an overview of two big chapters: lean on it for frameworks and key tables, and follow the page pointers into the DMG for the full detail.

**Source:** DMG Chapter 1, "A World of Your Own" (printed pages 9–42), and Chapter 2, "Creating a Multiverse" (printed pages 43–70).

> **Note on scope:** these two chapters are mostly campaign-design inspiration, not hard table rules. The numbers that *do* matter at the table — settlement population bands, tier level ranges, plane alignments — are pulled and verified below. The long descriptive passages (individual plane write-ups, sample factions, world-shaking-event tables) are summarized; go to the DMG for the prose.

---

## Chapter 1: A World of Your Own (DMG p.9–42)

A **campaign setting** is the world your adventures take place in — anything from a single town to a multiverse. You don't have to invent it all up front; build only what you need and let it grow as play continues (DMG p.9).

### The big picture (DMG p.9–10)

Before details, decide the broad strokes — how much do gods, magic, and monsters intrude on everyday life? The chapter frames these with "what if" questions (e.g., *Magic is everywhere* like Eberron, or *the gods walk the land — or are entirely absent* like Dark Sun). These choices set the tone everything else hangs on.

### Gods of your world (DMG p.10–12)

In rules terms it doesn't matter how many deities exist — **clerics choose domains, not deities** (Death, Knowledge, Life, Light, Nature, Tempest, Trickery, War), so you can map domains to gods however you like (DMG p.10). A quick way to build a pantheon: invent one god per cleric domain.

Religious structures the chapter describes (DMG p.10–12): **loose pantheons** (many gods of varied alignment, the D&D default — e.g., Greyhawk, Forgotten Realms), **tight pantheons** (one religion embracing a small family of gods, e.g., Norse), **mystery cults**, monotheism, dualism, and animism. It also ranks divine power: **greater deities** (remote, can't be summoned), **lesser deities** (embodied somewhere, can be encountered), and **quasi-deities** — demigods, titans, and vestiges.

*Full detail, plus the sample Dawn War pantheon table: DMG p.10–12.*

### Mapping your world (DMG p.14)

Work **top-down** (continent first, zoom in) or **bottom-up** (start local, zoom out). Hex paper suits outdoor maps. The chapter gives three nested scales:

| Scale | 1 hex = | A full-page map covers | Typical settlement density |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Province** | 1 mile | ~1 day's travel across in clear terrain | One town + 8–12 villages/hamlets (or a lone keep, or nothing) |
| **Kingdom** | 6 miles | ~the size of Great Britain / half of California | 8–12 cities or towns |
| **Continent** | 60 miles | Multiple kingdom-scale maps stitched together | 8–12 large cities (capitals, trade hubs) |

The scales nest cleanly: 1 continent hex = 10 kingdom hexes; 1 kingdom hex = 6 province hexes (DMG p.14).

### Settlements (DMG p.15–19)

Most D&D settlements are farming villages clustered around a larger town or city. Detail a settlement only as much as its purpose requires — local color (rest stop), home base, or adventure site. The three size bands with verified population caps (DMG p.16–17):

| Settlement | Population (about) | Government | Defense |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Village** | Up to ~1,000 | A non-resident noble rules; a resident **reeve** handles disputes and taxes | Small force or a citizen militia |
| **Town** | Up to ~6,000 | Resident noble + appointed lord mayor; elected council for the middle class | A sizable professional army |
| **City** | Up to ~25,000 | Resident noble(s), a lord mayor, and a powerful elected council; divided into wards | Professional army, guards, and town watch |

Cities above 25,000 are **extremely rare** — true metropolises (Waterdeep, Sharn, the Free City of Greyhawk) are vital beacons. Commerce scales with size: villages have basic supplies (often just an inn or trading post); towns have inns, taverns, and merchant guilds; cities have almost any good or service.

*Full detail (purpose, atmosphere, government, defenses): DMG p.15–19.*

### Languages and dialects (DMG p.20)

You can replace the PHB's default languages, split them into regional dialects, or add ancient tongues and secret languages (beyond Druidic and thieves' cant). Regional language differences can make diplomacy between kingdoms genuinely harder, and the conqueror's language can become a mark of social status (DMG p.20).

### Factions and organizations (DMG p.21–24)

Temples, guilds, orders, secret societies, and colleges anchor characters in the world as patrons, allies, or enemies — and keep higher-level PCs connected once their backgrounds fade in relevance. The chapter walks through **creating a faction** (role, goals, founder, members, symbol, motto) and gives the Forgotten Realms factions as samples (Harpers, Order of the Gauntlet, Emerald Enclave, Lords' Alliance, Zhentarim).

**Renown (optional rule, DMG p.22–23).** A numeric track, starting at 0, of a character's standing in *each* organization they belong to. Advancing the org's interests gives **+1 renown**; completing a mission specifically assigned by (or directly benefiting) the org gives **+2**. Renown can never drop below 0. Benefits the DM ties to renown thresholds: **rank/title**, friendlier member attitudes, and perks (contacts, safe houses, discounts, followers, called-in favors). **Piety** (DMG p.23) is a renown-style variant tracking a character's devotion to a specific deity.

*Full detail and the sample faction-rank table: DMG p.21–24.*

### Magic in your world (DMG p.23–24)

Decide how common and how regulated magic is — it shapes everything from prices to law. Key questions (DMG p.24): which magic is common vs. socially unacceptable vs. rare; how unusual spellcasters (especially high-level ones) are; how rare magic items, magical locations, and supernatural creatures are; and how authorities and common folk use and guard against magic. Answers cascade — common low-level spellcasting (Eberron) means common magical conveniences; a low-magic world (Dark Sun) makes magic feared and dangerous.

### Flavors of fantasy (DMG p.38–41)

Sub-genres you can flavor a campaign with (names + one-liners; DMG p.38–41):

- **Heroic fantasy** — the D&D baseline: many races, medieval-ish society, dungeon delves and monster-slaying.
- **Sword and sorcery** — grim, gritty, pulp; greedy antiheroes; magic is rare, decadent, and often villainous (Conan, Athas).
- **Epic fantasy** — good vs. evil writ large; the heroes are genuinely heroic; romance and moral struggle (Dragonlance).
- **Mythic fantasy** — themes of ancient myth and legend; demigod heroes, mighty labors, gods as patrons or obstacles (Gilgamesh, the Greek myths).
- **Dark fantasy** — horror spin: vampires, necromancers, werewolves; built on dread and pacing (Ravenloft). Discuss tone with players first.
- **Mystery** — PCs as investigators; puzzles and problem-solving alongside combat.
- **Intrigue** — politics, espionage, sabotage; social skills and contacts matter more than attack spells.
- **Swashbuckling** — pirates and musketeers; cities, courts, and ships over dungeons.
- **War** — heroes whose missions turn the tide of a larger conflict.
- **Wuxia** — Asian martial-arts flavor, achieved largely through re-skinning existing classes/spells (a ninja is a Way of Shadow monk; a samurai is an Oath of Devotion paladin).

You can also mix genres or fold in science fiction (**Crossing the Streams**, DMG p.41).

### Tiers of play (DMG p.36–38)

The four tiers are the natural milestones for escalating the scale of your campaign's threats. As characters gain power, the events they face must grow in magnitude — and powerful factions start treating them as threats or courting their alliance.

| Tier | Levels | Title | Scope & what they can do |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1** | 1–4 | **Local heroes** | Still learning their class; mundane gear and 1st–2nd-level spells. The fate of a **village** may hang on them. Fight orcs, wolves, cultists, ghouls; avoid even a young dragon. |
| **2** | 5–10 | **Heroes of the realm** | Master the basics; gain 3rd-level spells (fly, fireball) up through 5th. The fate of a **region** is at stake. Face giants, hydras, mind flayers, scheming devils; can challenge a young dragon. |
| **3** | 11–16 | **Masters of the realm** | True paragons; 6th–8th-level spells reshape how they adventure (teleport, true seeing). The fate of a **nation or the world** turns on their quests. Build fortresses, found orders, broker wars; face beholders, rakshasas, an adult dragon. |
| **4** | 17–20 | **Masters of the world** | Superheroic; 9th-level spells (wish, gate). Their deeds determine the fate of **millions across the Material Plane and beyond**; they traverse other planes and fight titans, archdevils, liches, even avatars of the gods. At 20th level they reach the pinnacle of mortal achievement (and can earn **epic boons** — DMG ch.7). |

*Full detail and the Starting Equipment table for higher-level starts: DMG p.36–38.*

### Creating a campaign (DMG p.25–26)

Start small — the players only need to know their **home base** (a small town or village at the edge of the wilderness is ideal) and maybe a nearby dungeon. The three setup steps (DMG p.25):

1. **Create a home base** (use the Settlements guidance).
2. **Create a local region** — a province-scale map (1 hex = 1 mile), home base near center, filled with 2–4 dungeons and 1–3 extra settlements within a day's travel (~25–30 miles).
3. **Craft a starting adventure** — a single dungeon works well (see ch.3).

Then **set the stage** with a short (≤2-page) campaign handout, and **involve the characters** by weaving their backgrounds into the campaign's story.

### Campaign events (DMG p.26–35)

**World-shaking events** — wars, disasters, invasions, assassinations — are the sparks that ignite and sustain a campaign, and the cure for a stagnating story. Place the biggest ones at the **beginning, middle, and end** of a story arc. The chapter offers a `d10` World-Shaking Events table for inspiration (DMG p.26): rise/fall of a leader or era, cataclysmic disaster, assault or invasion, rebellion, extinction/depletion, new organization, prediction/omen/prophecy, discovery/expansion/invention, and myth & legend — each with detailed prompts for fleshing it out.

**Tracking time (DMG p.32).** Use a calendar — even a real-world one to start — to track seasons, festivals, and the timing of these planned events; customize it for your world if you wish.

> **Between adventures** (downtime, lifestyle/expenses, building strongholds) is its own chapter — see DMG Chapter 6 (printed p.125), referenced here at DMG p.21–22.

## Chapter 2: Creating a Multiverse (DMG p.43–70)

The **planes of existence** are dimensions formed and governed by spiritual and elemental principles — not just other worlds. Higher-tier adventurers eventually travel among them. The **Material Plane** is where the worlds of D&D sit; the rest of the multiverse is defined in relation to it (DMG p.43).

### Planar categories (DMG p.43)

The default D&D cosmology groups its planes as follows:

- **The Material Plane and its echoes** — the **Feywild** and the **Shadowfell** are reflections of the Material Plane.
- **The Transitive Planes** — the **Ethereal** and **Astral** planes: mostly featureless, serving as pathways between planes.
- **The Inner Planes** — the four **Elemental Planes** (Air, Earth, Fire, Water) plus the **Elemental Chaos** that surrounds them; the raw physical/elemental substance of reality.
- **The Outer Planes** — **sixteen** planes corresponding to the eight non-neutral alignments and the shades between them; realms of spirituality and thought, home to celestials, fiends, and deities.
- **The Positive and Negative Planes** — these two enfold the rest of the cosmology, providing the raw forces of **life and death** that underlie all existence.

### The Material Plane and its echoes (DMG p.49–52)

- **The Feywild** (Plane of Faerie, DMG p.49) — a realm of everlasting twilight, soft lights, music, and death, existing in parallel to the Material Plane and mirroring its landscape in spectacular, exaggerated forms. Ruled by the Seelie (Summer) and Unseelie courts. Time can warp here — a visitor may return to find more or less time has passed (DMG p.50).
- **The Shadowfell** (DMG p.51) — a colorless, gloomy echo that overlaps the Material Plane the same way the Feywild does, turning its features into twisted, decayed reflections. Source of shadow and home to grim undead.

### The Transitive Planes (DMG p.46–48)

- **The Astral Plane** (DMG p.46) — the realm of thought and dream, a vast silvery sea you cross as a disembodied soul to reach the Outer Planes. Creatures don't age, hunger, or thirst. Reached chiefly by the **astral projection** spell (which keeps your body safe behind a **silver cord** — severing it means true death). **Color pools** are gateways to other planes (the *Astral Color Pools* table lists each plane's pool color, DMG p.47).
- **The Ethereal Plane** (DMG p.48) — a misty, fog-bound dimension. Its **Border Ethereal** overlaps the Material and Inner Planes (every location there has an Ethereal counterpart), making it ideal for invisible reconnaissance; the **Deep Ethereal** is the swirling-mist region between planes, crossed via colored curtains. Reached with **etherealness** or **plane shift**.

### The Inner Planes (DMG p.52–57)

The four **Elemental Planes** — Air, Earth, Fire, Water — form a ring around the Material Plane, suspended in the **Elemental Chaos**. Near the Material Plane the elements mingle into recognizable land/sea/sky (the hospitable "border regions" most travelers mean); farther out the elements grow pure and lethally hostile, ruled by elemental spirits and the Elemental Princes of Evil. The border regions *between* adjacent elemental planes are sometimes named as planes of their own (e.g., the **Frostfell**/Plane of Ice between Air and Water; the **Great Conflagration** between Air and Fire). The Elemental Chaos at the farthest extents dissolves all elements together.

### The Outer Planes (DMG p.57–66)

Sixteen planes arranged by alignment around the neutral **Outlands**. Those with an element of **good** are the **Upper Planes** (home of celestials); those with **evil** are the **Lower Planes** (home of fiends). A visitor whose alignment clashes with a plane's feels "dissonance." Most Outer Planes have **layers** (Mount Celestia has 7, the Nine Hells 9, the Abyss seemingly infinite).

| Outer Plane | Alignment | One-line |
|---|---|---|
| **Mount Celestia**, the Seven Heavens of | LG | Seven-tiered mountain of perfect order and good (Upper) |
| **Bytopia**, the Twin Paradises of | NG, LG | Two facing worlds of pastoral industry and benevolence (Upper) |
| **Elysium**, the Blessed Fields of | NG | The plane of pure goodness; goodness as a state of being (Upper) |
| **The Beastlands**, the Wilderness of | NG, CG | Untamed wilds where nature and animal spirit reign (Upper) |
| **Arborea**, the Olympian Glades of | CG | Wild, passionate beauty; home of the Greek-style gods (Upper) |
| **Ysgard**, the Heroic Domains of | CN, CG | Norse-flavored battlefields where the fallen rise each dawn (Upper) |
| **Limbo**, the Ever-Changing Chaos of | CN | Raw chaos shaped by will; home of the slaadi |
| **Pandemonium**, the Windswept Depths of | CN, CE | Endless screaming, wind-torn caverns that drive minds mad (Lower) |
| **The Abyss**, the Infinite Layers of | CE | Boundless layers of evil and entropy; home of demons (Lower) |
| **Carceri**, the Tarterian Depths of | NE, CE | The cosmic prison; betrayal and inescapable confinement (Lower) |
| **Hades**, the Gray Waste of | NE | Colorless despair that drains hope and will (Lower) |
| **Gehenna**, the Bleak Eternity of | NE, LE | Volcanic, ever-burning slopes of suspicion and greed (Lower) |
| **The Nine Hells of Baator** | LE | Nine layers of tyrannical, lawful evil; home of devils (Lower) |
| **Acheron**, the Infinite Battlefield of | LN, LE | Iron cubes colliding in eternal, ordered warfare (Lower) |
| **Mechanus**, the Clockwork Nirvana of | LN | Interlocking gears of absolute law; home of the modrons |
| **Arcadia**, the Peaceable Kingdoms of | LN, LG | Perfectly ordered, idyllic fields of disciplined good (Upper) |

(Each plane's full write-up includes an **optional plane rule** — e.g., the *psychic dissonance* exhaustion check for visiting an incompatible plane, DMG p.58.)

Two features connect the Outer Planes: the **River Styx** (winds through the top layers of all the Lower Planes; touching its water risks a *feeblemind* effect, DC 15 Int save) and the **Infinite Staircase** (an extradimensional spiral stair reachable through nondescript doors).

### Other planes (DMG p.67–68)

- **The Outlands and Sigil** (DMG p.67) — the Outlands is the disk-shaped **plane of neutrality** at the hub of the wheel; its rim holds **sixteen gate-towns**, each with a portal to one Outer Plane. At its center rises the Spire, atop which floats **Sigil, the City of Doors** — a ring-shaped metropolis riddled with portals to everywhere, ruled by the inscrutable **Lady of Pain**.
- **Demiplanes** (DMG p.68) — small extradimensional spaces with their own physical laws, created by spells (e.g., *Mordenkainen's magnificent mansion*) or existing naturally (e.g., Barovia in Ravenloft). Best reached via *gate*.
- **The Far Realm** (DMG p.68) — outside the known multiverse; an alien, maddening reality whose leaking energies warp matter and spawn aberrations (mind flayers, beholders). No reliable portals remain.

### Putting the planes together — the cosmological models (DMG p.43–45)

You choose which planes to include; at minimum a campaign needs origin planes for fiends, celestials, and elementals, a place for deities, an afterlife, and a way to travel between planes (DMG p.43). Several theoretical arrangements exist:

- **The Great Wheel** (the default, DMG p.44) — concentric wheels with the Material Plane and its echoes at the center, the Inner Planes around it (enveloped by the Ethereal), then the Outer Planes arranged by alignment in an outer wheel linked by the Outlands.
- **The World Tree** (DMG p.44) — planes set among the roots and branches of a cosmic tree (Norse Yggdrasil; the Forgotten Realms variant).
- **The World Axis** (DMG p.44) — the Material Plane between the Astral Sea (holding the Outer Planes) above and the Elemental Chaos (with the Abyss at its bottom) below.
- Plus alternatives: the **Omniverse**, **Myriad Planes**, the **Orrery** (Eberron), the **Winding Road**, **Mount Olympus**, the **Solar Barge** (Egyptian), **One World**, and **the Otherworld** (DMG p.44–45).

### Planar travel (DMG p.44–46)

Three broad methods:

- **Portals** (DMG p.45) — stationary links between specific locations on two planes; often guarded and gated by a requirement (time, situation, command word, or a **key** item — the basis of Sigil's nickname).
- **Spells** (DMG p.46) — *plane shift* and *gate* transport directly (plane shift needs a forked metal rod attuned to the destination, ≥250 gp); *etherealness* enters the Ethereal; *astral projection* projects you into the Astral Plane and on to the Outer Planes.
- **The Transitive Planes themselves** — crossing the **Astral** (via color pools) or the **Ethereal** (via curtains) to reach a target plane.

*Full detail, including portal requirements and the worlds of the Material Plane: DMG p.44–46, 68–70.*

## Common questions

**Q: Do I have to design my whole world before I start a campaign?**
A: No. Start small — a home base, a local region within a day's travel, and one starting adventure — and grow the world as play continues (DMG p.25).

**Q: How big is a "city" in D&D, and how rare are bigger ones?**
A: A city is up to about 25,000 people. Settlements above 25,000 are *extremely* rare — true metropolises like Waterdeep are landmark exceptions (DMG p.17).

**Q: My world has one god / no gods — does that break the cleric?**
A: No. Clerics choose **domains, not deities**, so the rules don't care how many gods exist; you map domains to gods (or to none) however you like (DMG p.10).

**Q: What can characters realistically affect at each tier?**
A: Levels 1–4 a village; 5–10 a region; 11–16 a nation or the world; 17–20 millions of lives across the Material Plane and beyond (DMG p.36–38).

**Q: How many Outer Planes are there, and how are they organized?**
A: Sixteen, by alignment, around the neutral Outlands — the good-aligned ones are the Upper Planes (celestials), the evil ones the Lower Planes (fiends), linked in the default "Great Wheel" (DMG p.43, 57).

**Q: How do you actually get to another plane?**
A: Via portals, spells (plane shift, gate, etherealness, astral projection), or by crossing the Transitive Planes — the Astral (color pools) or the Ethereal (curtains) (DMG p.44–46).

**Q: What's the difference between the Astral and the Ethereal?**
A: The Astral is the silvery "thought" sea you cross to reach the **Outer** Planes (via astral projection and color pools); the Ethereal is the misty plane overlapping the **Material and Inner** Planes (via etherealness/plane shift), great for invisible movement (DMG p.46, 48).
