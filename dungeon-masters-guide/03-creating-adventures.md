# Creating Adventures

How to design a D&D adventure as a story (premise, villain, structure), the difference between location-based and event-based adventures, and — most importantly for the DM at the table — the math for building and gauging combat encounters: XP thresholds, the encounter multiplier, and the adventuring-day budget.

**Source:** DMG Chapter 3, "Creating Adventures" (printed pages 71–88).

> **Quick lookup for the encounter math:** [`../quick-reference/encounter-building.md`](../quick-reference/encounter-building.md) has the same tables in cheat-sheet form. Use that file at the table; use this one for the full chapter context.

---

## What makes an adventure (DMG p.71–72)

An adventure is a story — a single "issue" or "episode," where a whole campaign is the series. A good adventure blends **exploration, social interaction, and combat** into a unified whole rather than a disjointed series of encounters.

The DMG lists the hallmarks of a great adventure (DMG p.71–72):

- **A credible threat** — a villain, a monster, a villain with lackeys, or an evil organization. Whatever it is, the antagonists should have **goals the heroes can uncover and thwart**.
- **Familiar tropes with clever twists** — dragons, orcs, mad wizards, and tavern hooks are fine staples, as long as you occasionally put a spin on them.
- **A clear focus on the present** — let the world's history show in the present situation; the adventure is about the here and now, not a lore dump.
- **Heroes who matter** — allow more than one outcome so players don't feel railroaded. If a villain appears before the end, the heroes should be able to beat that villain there.
- **Something for all player types** — balance exploration, social, and combat. A home adventure only has to appeal to *your* players, not every abstract player type.
- **Surprises** — occasional twists (a hidden tomb, odd monster pairings, surprise reinforcements, a red herring) keep players thinking, but don't overdo it.
- **Useful maps** — branching dungeons and landmark-rich wilderness give players meaningful choices.

**Published vs. homemade (DMG p.72).** A published adventure ships with maps, NPCs, monsters, and treasure ready to run (e.g., the D&D Starter Set). You can adjust it to fit your campaign — swap in a villain the party already knows, add personal hooks, or repurpose just a map or set piece. Even unused, published adventures are good idea fuel.

*Full detail: DMG p.71–72.*

## Adventure structure (DMG p.72)

Like any story, a typical adventure has a beginning, a middle, and an end:

- **Beginning** — a **hook** that gives the characters a compelling reason to get involved (they stumble onto something, get attacked, an assassin strikes, a dragon appears at the gates). Make it exciting and focused, with a clear sense of where the story is headed.
- **Middle** — where the bulk of the story unfolds. Each new challenge forces choices that affect the conclusion; secrets discovered along the way can reveal new goals. Meanwhile the adversaries pursue their own plans. Keep the characters as actors, never mere spectators.
- **Ending** — the **climax**, where tension peaks and the outcome hinges on the characters' actions. It need not tie up every thread; leaving a little unfinished business is an easy bridge to the next adventure.

*Full detail: DMG p.72.*

## Adventure types: location-based vs. event-based (DMG p.72–80)

The DMG frames two ways to build an adventure. Both come with d20/d12 idea tables you can roll on or pick from; the tables are random-inspiration aids — the steps below summarize the *method*, not the table contents.

### Location-based (DMG p.72–75)

Set in dungeons and remote wilderness — the cornerstone of countless campaigns. Build one in steps (order is flexible):

1. **Identify the party's goals** — why they go there (Dungeon Goals, Wilderness Goals, and Other Goals tables, DMG p.73–74). Examples: stop raiders, foil a scheme, retrieve a stolen item, rescue a captive, slay a dragon.
2. Choose the **villains, allies, patrons, and locations** (more idea tables).
3. **Key the map** — for each numbered area, the key describes its physical features and any encounter that plays out there, turning a sketch into a sequence of designed encounters.

### Event-based (DMG p.75–80)

The focus is on **what the characters and villains do** and what results; *where* it happens is secondary. More work than a location-based adventure, but the process follows steps:

1. **Start with a villain** (Adventure Villains table; flesh it out using Chapter 4). The villain drives the story.
2. **Determine the villain's actions** — build a timeline of what the villain does and when, *assuming no interference* from the party (Event-Based Villain Actions table: big event, crime spree, growing corruption, serial crimes, step by step, etc.).
3. Set the **event-based goals** and details (Event-Based Goals table).

The chapter also covers **mysteries** and **intrigue adventures** (DMG p.79–80) as special forms of event-based adventure — intrigue can feature many villains or no clear villain at all, and handles villains and clues differently from a standard plot.

*Full detail: DMG p.72–80.*

## Creating Encounters (DMG p.81)

Encounters are the individual scenes of the adventure. First, an encounter should be **fun for the players**; second, it shouldn't be a **burden to run**. A good one has a clear objective, ties into the larger story, and accounts for **three possible outcomes** — success, partial success, or failure — each with consequences so wins and losses matter.

**Character objectives (DMG p.81).** Players who don't know what they're meant to do get bored. Give each encounter a transparent objective. The DMG lists sample objectives you can reuse and stack into larger problems: *Make Peace, Protect an NPC or Object, Retrieve an Object, Run a Gauntlet, Sneak In, Stop a Ritual, Take Out a Single Target* — each with suggested complications.

*Full detail: DMG p.81.*

## Combat encounter difficulty — the math (DMG p.82)

This is the load-bearing part of the chapter. Every number below is reproduced exactly from the DMG and matches the quick-reference cheat sheet.

### The four difficulty categories (DMG p.82)

- **Easy** — doesn't tax resources; victory is essentially guaranteed.
- **Medium** — one or two scary moments, but no casualties; someone may need to spend healing.
- **Hard** — could go badly; weaker characters might be taken out, slim chance of a death.
- **Deadly** — could be lethal for one or more PCs; survival needs good tactics; the party risks defeat.

### The procedure (DMG p.82)

1. **Determine each character's XP thresholds.** Look up every PC's level on the table below — four thresholds per level (Easy / Medium / Hard / Deadly).
2. **Determine the party's XP threshold.** Add up all PCs' values in each column → four party totals. **Record these; they're reusable for the whole adventure.**
3. **Total the monsters' XP.** Add the XP value from each monster's stat block.
4. **Modify for multiple monsters.** Multiply the monsters' total XP by the **encounter multiplier** (below) based on the number of monsters. This **adjusted XP** is *only* a difficulty gauge — it is **not** the XP awarded to players.
5. **Compare.** The **closest party threshold that is lower than the adjusted XP** is the encounter's difficulty.

> When calculating, **don't count any monster whose CR is significantly below the average CR** of the group, unless you judge the weak monsters meaningfully add to the difficulty (DMG p.82).

### Encounter Multipliers (DMG p.82)

The more monsters, the more attack rolls land on the party each round, so a given XP total grows more dangerous. Multiply the monsters' **total** base XP by:

| Number of Monsters | Multiplier |
|---|---|
| 1 | ×1 |
| 2 | ×1.5 |
| 3–6 | ×2 |
| 7–10 | ×2.5 |
| 11–14 | ×3 |
| 15 or more | ×4 |

### Party-size adjustment (DMG p.83)

The multiplier table assumes a party of **three to five** characters.

- **Fewer than 3 PCs:** use the **next-higher** multiplier (e.g., 1 monster → ×1.5; 15+ monsters → ×5).
- **6 or more PCs:** use the **next-lower** multiplier (e.g., 1 monster → ×0.5).

The party thresholds themselves still come from summing each PC's value, so a bigger party also has a bigger budget.

### A note on Challenge Rating (DMG p.82)

Be cautious with monsters whose CR is above the party's average level, especially at low levels — a single big hit (an ogre, CR 2, can one-shot a 1st-level wizard) or a hard-to-overcome feature (a rakshasa, CR 13, is immune to 6th-level-and-lower spells) can make an encounter far tougher than its math suggests.

### Worked example (DMG p.82–83)

Party: **three 3rd-level PCs and one 2nd-level PC**. Sum each column:

| | Easy | Medium | Hard | Deadly |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 3× L3 + 1× L2 | **275** (75+75+75+50) | **550** (150+150+150+100) | **825** (225+225+225+150) | **1,400** (400+400+400+200) |

Now an encounter of **one bugbear + three hobgoblins** (four monsters). Their base XP totals 500; four monsters fall in the **3–6** band → **×2** → **adjusted 1,000 XP**. Compare 1,000 to the party's totals: it clears Hard (825) but not Deadly (1,400) → this is a **Hard** encounter.

*Full detail: DMG p.82–83.*

## Multipart encounters (DMG p.83)

If enemies arrive in **waves** the party doesn't face all at once, treat **each wave as a separate encounter** for difficulty. But note: the party **can't short rest between waves** (no Hit Dice, no short-rest abilities recovered). As a rule, if a multipart encounter's adjusted XP exceeds **one-third of the party's adventuring-day XP** (below), it will play tougher than the sum of its parts.

## Building encounters on a budget (DMG p.83–84)

Run the math backward. The party's thresholds are an **XP budget**: to build a target difficulty, pick monsters whose **adjusted** XP lands between that difficulty's threshold and the next-higher one — remembering that groups of monsters eat more budget than their base XP shows (step 4).

Example (same party as above): a **Medium** encounter needs adjusted XP **at least 550** (Medium total) and **under 825** (Hard total). A single CR 3 monster (700 XP) fits; so do **two dire wolves** (200 XP each → ×1.5 → 600 adjusted). Appendix B of the DMG lists every Monster Manual monster by CR to help (DMG p.84).

## The adventuring day (DMG p.84)

Assuming typical conditions and average luck, most parties can handle about **six to eight medium-or-hard encounters per day** before a long rest. More easy encounters → they can do more; more deadly ones → fewer.

To budget a whole day, add each character's **Adventuring Day XP** value. The total is a rough estimate of the **adjusted** monster XP the party can chew through before needing a long rest.

### Short rests (DMG p.84)

Over a full adventuring day, the party will likely take **two short rests**, about **one-third and two-thirds** of the way through. Pace encounters so those rests fall naturally.

## Modifying encounter difficulty (DMG p.84–85)

Location and situation shift difficulty without changing the monsters. Each **drawback the characters have that their enemies don't** bumps difficulty **up one step** (e.g., Easy → Medium); each **benefit they have that enemies don't** drops it **one step**. A benefit and a drawback cancel out.

Sample **drawbacks** (DMG p.85): the whole party is surprised and the enemy isn't; the enemy has cover and the party doesn't; the characters can't see the enemy; the characters take damage every round from an environmental or magical source the enemy doesn't; the characters are hampered in mobility (hanging from a rope, scaling a cliff, stuck to the floor). **Benefits** are the mirror image, favoring the characters.

**Fun combat encounters (DMG p.85)** are spiced up by terrain that risks both sides (frayed rope bridges, green slime), changes of elevation (pits, ledges, balconies), features that force movement (chandeliers, oil kegs, blade traps), enemies in hard-to-reach spots that force ranged attackers to reposition, and different monster types working together.

*Full detail: DMG p.84–85.*

## Random encounters (DMG p.85–87)

Random encounters deliver the unexpected as the party explores wilderness or a dungeon — usually a die roll against a table. Well-designed, they serve real purposes (DMG p.85): **create urgency** (rolling behind the screen works even with no result), **establish atmosphere** (thematically linked creatures set a tone), **drain resources**, **provide assistance** (some are helpful), **add interest** / foreshadow, and **reinforce campaign themes**. They should never feel like time-wasters; if they don't work for your table, don't use them.

**Triggering (DMG p.85).** Consider one when players go off track, when the party rests, on long uneventful journeys, or when the characters draw attention they shouldn't.

**Checking (DMG p.86).** Check roughly once an hour, every 4–8 hours, or once per day and once per long rest — whatever fits the area's activity. If rolling, use a **d20: on 18 or higher, an encounter occurs**, then roll on the appropriate table (reroll nonsensical results). The chapter also gives guidance and sample tables for **creating random encounter tables** (DMG p.86–87) — those big tables aren't reproduced here; consult the DMG directly when you need them.

*Full detail: DMG p.85–87.*

## Common questions

**Q: Is the adjusted XP what I award the players?**
A: No. The multiplier exists only to gauge difficulty. Players earn the monsters' **base** XP total (DMG p.82).

**Q: Why does the same XP get harder with more monsters?**
A: More monsters means more attack rolls per round against the party — action economy tilts against the PCs. The multiplier accounts for that (DMG p.82).

**Q: My party has only 2 PCs / has 6+ PCs — does anything change?**
A: Yes. Always sum the actual PCs' thresholds for the party budget. Then for the multiplier: fewer than 3 PCs → use the next-higher multiplier; 6 or more → use the next-lower (DMG p.82–83).

**Q: How many encounters should a day hold?**
A: About **6–8 medium-or-hard** encounters, budgeted with the Adventuring Day XP table, with **two short rests** at roughly the one-third and two-thirds marks (DMG p.84).

**Q: How do I build to a target difficulty instead of grading an existing one?**
A: Treat the party threshold as a budget — pick monsters whose **adjusted** XP lands between the target difficulty's threshold and the next-higher one (DMG p.83–84).

**Q: A weak monster is padding the encounter — do I count it?**
A: Ignore any monster whose CR is *significantly* below the group's average CR, unless you judge it meaningfully adds to the difficulty (DMG p.82).

**Q: Enemies arrive in waves — how do I rate that?**
A: Treat each wave as a separate encounter, but remember there's **no short rest between waves**; if a wave's adjusted XP tops one-third of the daily budget, it'll feel tougher than its grade (DMG p.83).
