# Creating Nonplayer Characters

How to flesh out the people, monsters, and villains the DM controls — the quick one-detail approach versus the full ten-sentence profile, how to give an NPC game statistics, the rules for NPCs who travel with the party (including the optional Loyalty score), the cast of contacts/hirelings/extras, and how to build a memorable villain (scheme, methods, secret weakness) plus the two evil class options.

**Source:** DMG Chapter 4, "Creating Nonplayer Characters" (printed pages 89–98).

> An NPC is *any* character controlled by the DM — friend or foe, innkeeper or dragon. Most are bit players; the adventurers are the stars. An NPC needs combat stats **only if it poses a threat** (DMG p.89).

---

## Designing NPCs (DMG p.89–92)

### Quick NPCs — one or two distinguishing details (DMG p.89)

Most NPCs need only **one or two memorable qualities**, not a full write-up — the no-nonsense blacksmith with the black-rose tattoo, or the badly dressed bard with the broken nose. A single distinguishing characteristic is usually enough to make an NPC stick in the players' minds.

### Detailed NPCs — the ten-sentence profile (DMG p.89–91)

For NPCs with bigger roles, the DMG suggests summing them up in **ten sentences, one for each element**. Each element has its own d-table of random suggestions you can roll on or simply pick from. The *categories* are what matter; the full tables are in the DMG.

| # | Element | What the sentence captures | Sample table entries |
|---|---------|----------------------------|----------------------|
| 1 | **Occupation and history** | Job plus a hint of the past | Served in an army; imprisoned for a crime; adventured years ago |
| 2 | **Appearance** (d20) | Most distinctive physical feature | Distinctive jewelry; pronounced scar; tattoos; unusual eye color; exceptionally beautiful/ugly |
| 3 | **Abilities** (d6 high / d6 low) | One above- or below-average trait | Strong as an ox; dim-witted; born leader; sickly |
| 4 | **Talent** (d20) | Something special they can do | Plays an instrument; perfect memory; great with animals; knows thieves' cant |
| 5 | **Mannerism** (d20) | A memorable verbal/physical quirk | Speaks in rhyme; uses colorful oaths; fidgets; twirls hair |
| 6 | **Interactions with others** (d12) | Default attitude (can shift by audience) | Friendly; arrogant; rude; suspicious; honest |
| 7 | **Useful knowledge** | One thing they know that helps the PCs | The best inn in town; a clue to a murder |
| 8 | **Ideal** (d6 by alignment) | A driving principle | Good: charity, greater good · Evil: domination, greed · Lawful: honor · Chaotic: freedom · plus Neutral and "Other" (glory, knowledge, redemption) |
| 9 | **Bond** (d10) | People/places/things they cherish | Protective of family; loyal to a patron; out for revenge; drawn to a special place |
| 10 | **Flaw or secret** (d12) | A weakness or hidden shame | Overpowering greed; a specific phobia; a secret crime; has a powerful enemy |

PCs who **uncover an NPC's ideal, bond, or flaw** can use it to influence the NPC in social interaction (see Chapter 8). Ideal-alignment links are suggestions only — an evil character can still hold "beauty" as an ideal (DMG p.90–91).

> **Sidebar — Monsters as NPCs (DMG p.91).** A named monster with a real role in the adventure deserves the same treatment as a humanoid NPC: a quirk of appearance plus an ideal, a bond, and a flaw. The DMG's worked example is the Xanathar (a beholder crime lord) — ideal: greed; bond: its lair beneath Waterdeep; flaw: a weakness for exotic pleasures.

*Full detail: DMG p.89–91.*

## NPC statistics (DMG p.92)

When an NPC needs game stats, there are **three options** (DMG p.92):

1. **Give it only the few statistics it needs.** Non-combatants rarely need a full stat block — just whatever the situation calls for.
2. **Use a monster stat block.** Appendix B of the *Monster Manual* has generic NPC stat blocks (commoner, guard, knight, mage, etc.) you can reskin or customize. Guidelines for adjusting them and building new ones are in **Chapter 9, "Dungeon Master's Workshop"** (DMG p.92; the NPC Features / "skin a stat block onto a race" rules live at DMG p.282).
3. **Give it a class and levels.** Build the NPC like a PC using the *Player's Handbook*; you can even use a character sheet. When you build an NPC this way you may **skip the background and instead pick two skill proficiencies** (DMG p.282).

For option 3, the DMG notes (p.92):

- **Class options:** two extra options exist for evil PCs/NPCs — the **Death domain** (cleric) and the **Oathbreaker** (paladin), both detailed at the end of this chapter (see below).
- **Equipment:** most NPCs don't need an exhaustive list; a combat enemy needs weapons, armor, and any treasure or magic items it might use against the party.
- **Challenge rating:** a combat-built NPC needs a CR — calculate it with the Chapter 9 rules, exactly as for a monster you design.

*Full detail: DMG p.92.*

## NPC party members (DMG p.92–93)

NPCs may join the party for a **share of the loot** (accepting an equal share of risk) or out of **loyalty, gratitude, or love**. Key rules (DMG p.92):

- The DM controls them, but **may transfer control to a player**. Even then the DM keeps the NPC a character in its own right, not a servant the players exploit.
- **Any NPC accompanying the party acts as a party member and earns a full share of XP**, and **must be counted when calculating encounter difficulty** (Chapter 3).

**Low-level followers (DMG p.92–93).** A campaign may let PCs take on lower-level NPCs (a paladin's squire, a wizard's apprentice, etc.). Upside: backup characters if a PC retires or dies. Downside: more bodies to track, and the followers level *faster* (equal XP shares, smaller burden) while the PCs advance a bit slower. Powerful, level-appropriate monsters can one-shot a low-level follower, so the party should expect to spend effort and healing protecting them.

**Adventurer NPCs (DMG p.93).** Short a player or two? Fill the ranks with NPCs at the **lowest party member's level**, built (ideally by the players) with PHB rules. Keep them easy to run by limiting class options — good picks: **Life-domain cleric, Champion fighter, Thief rogue, Evocation wizard**. If a player isn't playing one true to its personality, the DM can take it back, hand it to someone else, or write it out of the party.

### Optional rule: Loyalty (DMG p.93)

A numerical alternative to roleplaying how far an NPC party member will go for the group. **All values below are exact.**

- **Scale:** loyalty runs from **0 to 20**.
- **Maximum loyalty score** = the **highest Charisma score** among all the adventurers.
- **Starting loyalty score** = **half** that maximum. If the highest Charisma changes (a PC dies or leaves), adjust the NPC's score accordingly.
- **Track it in secret** so players can't be sure whether an NPC is loyal — even one currently under a player's control.

**Raising loyalty** (never above its maximum):

- **+1d4** if other party members help the NPC achieve a goal tied to its **bond**.
- **+1d4** if the NPC is treated particularly well (e.g., gifted a magic weapon) or is **rescued** by another party member.

**Lowering loyalty** (never below 0):

- **−1d4** when other members act counter to the NPC's **alignment or bond**.
- **−2d4** if the NPC is **abused, misled, or endangered** by the party for purely selfish reasons.

**What the score means:**

- **10 or higher** — the NPC will **risk life and limb** for fellow party members.
- **1 to 10** — loyalty is **tenuous**.
- **0** — the NPC is **no longer loyal** and no longer acts in the party's best interests: it either **leaves** (attacking anyone who tries to stop it) or works **in secret to bring about the party's downfall**.

*Full detail: DMG p.92–93.*

## Contacts, patrons, hirelings, and extras (DMG p.93–94)

- **Contacts (DMG p.93–94)** — NPCs with close ties to one or more PCs. They don't adventure, but supply information, rumors, goods, or advice (free or paid). Casual contacts need just a name and a detail; flesh out recurring ones, especially potential allies or enemies — give them goals. Some PHB backgrounds grant starting contacts.
- **Patrons (DMG p.94)** — a contact who **employs** the party, providing rewards, quests, and adventure hooks, and who usually has a vested interest in the PCs' success (a retired adventurer, a mayor facing a dragon, a sheriff offering a bounty, a noble clearing an estate).
- **Hirelings (DMG p.94)** — NPCs paid for services; the rules live in **PHB Chapter 5, "Equipment"** (skilled hirelings ~2 gp/day, unskilled ~2 sp/day per the PHB). Most need little development and may go unnamed (a coach driver, a messenger), though a long-term hire like a ship captain can grow into an ally, patron, or enemy. **Long-term hires add to the PCs' lifestyle expenses** (see Chapter 6, "Between Adventures").
- **Extras (DMG p.94)** — background characters the PCs rarely interact with. Be ready to invent a name and mannerism on the fly, since players can elevate an extra to a central figure at any moment (plunder the PHB race-name lists in a pinch).

*Full detail: DMG p.93–94.*

## Villains (DMG p.94–98)

Chapter 3 helps you pick a suitable villain; this section fleshes out the villain's **scheme, methods, and weakness**. The DMG provides idea tables for each — paraphrased categories and a few examples below; roll or pick.

### The villain's scheme (DMG p.94–95)

Roll **d8** for the broad objective, then a sub-table for the specific scheme:

| d8 | Objective | Example schemes |
|----|-----------|------------------|
| 1 | **Immortality** (d4) | Acquire a life-prolonging legendary item; ascend to godhood; become undead |
| 2 | **Influence** (d4) | Seize a title; win a tournament; place a pawn in power |
| 3 | **Magic** (d6) | Obtain an artifact; build a magical device; open a gate to another world |
| 4 | **Mayhem** (d6) | Fulfill an apocalyptic prophecy; overthrow a government; trigger a natural disaster |
| 5 | **Passion** (d4) | Restore a dead loved one; prove worthy of someone's love; destroy a rival |
| 6 | **Power** (d4) | Conquer a region; seize an army; become the power behind the throne |
| 7 | **Revenge** (d4) | Avenge a humiliation, an injury, or a loved one's death |
| 8 | **Wealth** (d4) | Control trade or resources; marry into wealth; plunder ruins |

### The villain's methods (DMG p.95)

A large **d20** table of categories, most with a sub-table, covering how the villain pursues the scheme:

- **Violence & death:** Agricultural devastation, Murder (assassination, poisoning…), Execution, Torture, Warfare (ambush, invasion, massacre…)
- **Coercion & control:** Captivity/coercion (kidnapping, slavery, threats…), Confidence scams, Defamation, Vice
- **Society & rule:** Politics (espionage, genocide, oppression…), Religion (curses, desecration, false gods, cults), Theft/property crime (arson, blackmail, smuggling…)
- **Supernatural:** Magical mayhem (hauntings, illusions, mind control, raising the dead, weather control, summoning monsters)

### The villain's secret weakness (DMG p.96)

A smart villain hides its weakness — a lich's phylactery is the archetype. Roll **d8**:

| d8 | Weakness |
|----|----------|
| 1 | A hidden object holds the villain's soul |
| 2 | Its power breaks if the death of its true love is avenged |
| 3 | It is weakened in the presence of a particular artifact |
| 4 | A special weapon deals extra damage against it |
| 5 | It is destroyed if it speaks its true name |
| 6 | An ancient prophecy or riddle reveals how it can be overthrown |
| 7 | It falls when an ancient enemy forgives its past actions |
| 8 | It loses its power if a mystic bargain it struck long ago is completed |

### Building a villain's stat block (DMG p.92–93, 96)

Use the NPC statistics options above. A villain meant to be fought needs weapons, armor, treasure/magic items, and a calculated CR (Chapter 9). Significant villains also deserve the full ten-sentence treatment — and an *interesting* villain often has both a scheme the PCs can uncover and a concealed weakness they can exploit, which is "very gratifying for players" (DMG p.96).

### Villainous class options (DMG p.96–98)

Two evil archetypes a player can take **with DM approval**, or that you can give an NPC:

- **Cleric: Death domain (DMG p.96–97)** — necromancy and negative-energy clerics (patrons such as Myrkul, Wee Jas). Features: bonus *martial weapon* proficiency; **Reaper** (a chosen necromancy cantrip can target two creatures within 5 ft of each other); **Channel Divinity: Touch of Death** (extra necrotic damage = 5 + twice cleric level on a melee hit); **Inescapable Destruction** (necrotic damage ignores resistance) at 6th; **Divine Strike** (+1d8, later +2d8, necrotic on a weapon hit) at 8th; **Improved Reaper** (twin 1st–5th-level necromancy spells) at 17th. Death Domain spells at 1/3/5/7/9: *false life, ray of sickness; blindness/deafness, ray of enfeeblement; animate dead, vampiric touch; blight, death ward; antilife shell, cloudkill*.
- **Paladin: Oathbreaker (DMG p.97–98)** — a fallen paladin (**must be evil and at least 3rd level**) who replaces Sacred Oath features. Channel Divinity: **Control Undead** and **Dreadful Aspect** (30-ft frighten); **Aura of Hate** (allies' fiends/undead gain Cha-mod melee damage) at 7th; **Supernatural Resistance** to nonmagical bludgeoning/piercing/slashing at 15th; **Dread Lord** (aura of gloom, 4d10 psychic to frightened enemies, shadow attacks) at 20th. Oathbreaker spells at 3/5/9/13/17: *hellish rebuke, inflict wounds; crown of madness, darkness; animate dead, bestow curse; blight, confusion; contagion, dominate person*. A sidebar (**Oathbreaker Atonement**, DMG p.98) lets the DM allow a once-only return to a true paladin after the character sheds evil and completes a quest.

*Full detail: DMG p.94–98.*

## Common questions

**Q: Does an NPC need a full stat block?**
A: Only if it poses a threat. Non-combatants need at most a few situational statistics (DMG p.89, 92).

**Q: An NPC is traveling with us — does it get XP, and does it affect encounter difficulty?**
A: Yes to both. Any NPC party member earns a **full share of XP** and **must be counted** when gauging encounter difficulty (DMG p.92).

**Q: What sets an NPC's maximum and starting Loyalty score?**
A: Maximum = the **highest Charisma score** in the party; starting = **half** of that. If the highest Charisma changes, adjust the score (DMG p.93).

**Q: How much does loyalty move, and when?**
A: **+1d4** for helping the NPC's bond, or treating it well / rescuing it. **−1d4** for acting against its alignment or bond; **−2d4** for selfishly abusing, misleading, or endangering it. Never above its max or below 0 (DMG p.93).

**Q: What happens at Loyalty 0?**
A: The NPC stops acting in the party's interest — it either leaves (fighting anyone who interferes) or secretly works toward the party's downfall (DMG p.93).

**Q: Who controls an NPC party member?**
A: The DM, who may hand control to a player — but the DM still ensures the NPC is played as its own character, and can reclaim it if it's mishandled (DMG p.92–93).

**Q: How do I make a monster feel like a real villain rather than a stat block?**
A: Give it appearance, a quirk, and especially an **ideal, a bond, and a flaw**, then use the scheme / methods / secret-weakness tables (DMG p.91, 94–96).

**Q: Where are the rules for building or reskinning a villain's stat block and its CR?**
A: Chapter 4 points to **Chapter 9, "Dungeon Master's Workshop"** (NPC Features and stat-block creation, DMG p.282) (DMG p.92).

**Q: Can a player play a Death-domain cleric or an Oathbreaker paladin?**
A: Yes, with DM approval. The Oathbreaker additionally requires the paladin to be **evil and at least 3rd level** (DMG p.92, 96–98).
