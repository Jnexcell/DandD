# Between Adventures

How to stitch individual adventures into a campaign (story arcs, foreshadowing, campaign tracking), the recurring costs of property and hirelings, and the DMG's set of optional **downtime activities** — what each one costs, how long it takes, the check involved, and the outcome.

**Source:** DMG Chapter 6, "Between Adventures" (printed pages 125–132).

> **Scope note:** The DMG points to the PHB for two things this chapter builds on — **lifestyle expenses** (PHB Chapter 5, p.157) and the *basic* **downtime activities** (Crafting, Practicing a Profession, Recuperating, Researching, Training; PHB Chapter 8, p.187). Those PHB pieces are summarized below for context; the *new* material — recurring expenses and the expanded/optional downtime set — is the DMG's own.

---

## Linking adventures into a campaign (DMG p.125)

A campaign is more than a string of adventures; it's also the lulls between them, when characters spend treasure and pursue personal goals (DMG p.125).

- **Episodic vs. serialized (DMG p.125).** An episodic campaign (like a monster-of-the-week TV show) needs no story links: each adventure has its own villains and ties up its own threads, and the next is unrelated. It's the easiest style to run — just find or build level-appropriate adventures. A **serialized** campaign with an overarching narrative makes players feel their choices have far-reaching consequences; a few simple modifications let early adventures set up later ones.
- **Overarching story (DMG p.125).** Two classic framings, which are really the same good-vs-evil story told two ways:
  - *The Quest of Many Parts* — one goal reachable only by completing a chain of related quests (e.g., clearing the nine dungeons of the Nine Dread Princes, or gathering scattered artifact fragments before reassembling them to beat a cosmic threat).
  - *Agents of X* — the party serves something larger than themselves (a kingdom, a secret order, a faith), motivated by loyalty: mapping an uncharted region, seeking a fallen empire's capital, defending the last bastions of civilization, or working as spies and assassins.
- **Planting adventure seeds (DMG p.125–126).** Make the campaign feel like one story by seeding the *next* adventure before the current one ends — a vision from a magic fountain, a cryptic map or relic, an NPC's plea, a wanted poster, an anonymous letter, evidence found on a villain's corpse. The trick is finesse: the lure should be compelling but not so irresistible that players stop caring about the adventure at hand. Save your best seeds for the very end of an adventure or for downtime.
- **Foreshadowing (DMG p.126).** Subtler than a seed — hint at future threats without telegraphing them. Examples: an unknown organization's symbol on an enemy's gear, a street prophet's fragmentary prophecy, a politically fraught royal marriage, bugbear scouts heralding a hobgoblin invasion, NPC adventurers being murdered in an unusual way. Not all foreshadowing pays off, and that's fine.

*Full detail: DMG p.125–126.*

## Campaign tracking (DMG p.126)

Consistency makes the world feel real: dead monsters stay dead (unless raised), looted rooms stay empty, open doors stay open. Since memory is fallible, keep records — jot notes on the adventure map for open doors and disarmed traps, and keep a dedicated campaign notebook (physical or electronic) for everything larger. The DMG suggests it might hold:

- **Campaign Planner** — the main story arc and ideas you hope to use later; update as you go.
- **Character Notes** — PC backgrounds and goals (for tailoring hooks), plus a running tally of classes/levels and any active quests or downtime activities. Record any ship or stronghold and its hirelings.
- **Player Handouts** — keep a copy of everything you hand out so you needn't recall its contents.
- **Adventure Log** — an "episode guide": a summary of each session/adventure. You can share an edited (secret-free) version with players.
- **NPC Notes** — stats and roleplaying notes (voice, home, work, family, secret) for any NPC the party meets more than once.
- **Campaign Calendar** — track the passage of time, seasons, holidays, and story-relevant events; noticing time pass makes the world feel real.
- **Toolbox** — save every monster, magic item, trap, map, or encounter you create or alter, so you can reuse it.

*Full detail: DMG p.126.*

## Recurring expenses (DMG p.126–127)

Beyond lifestyle costs, adventurers who own property, run businesses, or employ hirelings have ongoing drains on their income.

### Lifestyle expenses (PHB Chapter 5, p.157 — the table the DMG points to)

The DMG defers to the PHB for day-to-day living costs. This is the **PHB Lifestyle Expenses table** (PHB p.157), reproduced for convenience. Costs are **per day**; multiply by 30 for a 30-day stretch.

| Lifestyle | Price/Day |
|---|---|
| Wretched | — (free; lived in inhumane conditions) |
| Squalid | 1 sp |
| Poor | 2 sp |
| Modest | 1 gp |
| Comfortable | 2 gp |
| Wealthy | 4 gp |
| Aristocratic | 10 gp minimum |

A character's **normal residence** is covered by lifestyle expenses, so it is *not* listed on the Maintenance Costs table below (DMG p.127).

### Maintenance Costs — property (DMG p.127)

When adventurers come to own a castle, tavern, or other property (common after about 10th level), the **Maintenance Costs** table gives the per-day upkeep, including hireling salaries. **Maintenance is paid every 30 days** (i.e., 30 × the per-day cost), and the staff is assumed to include a steward who can pay in the party's absence. If the property earns money (fees, tithes, sales), that income is already netted into the listed cost. "Skilled" vs. "untrained" hirelings are defined in the PHB (PHB p.159).

| Property | Total Cost/Day | Skilled Hirelings | Untrained Hirelings |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Abbey | 20 gp | 5 | 25 |
| Farm | 5 sp | 1 | 2 |
| Guildhall, town or city | 5 gp | 5 | 3 |
| Inn, rural (roadside) | 10 gp | 5 | 10 |
| Inn, town or city | 10 gp | 5 | 10 |
| Keep or small castle | 4 gp | 5 | 25 |
| Lodge, hunting | 5 sp | 1 | 3 |
| Noble estate | 10 gp | 3 | 15 |
| Outpost or fort | 50 gp | 20 | 40 |
| Palace or large castle | 400 gp | 200 | 100 |
| Shop | 2 gp | 1 | — |
| Temple, large | 25 gp | 10 | 10 |
| Temple, small | 1 gp | 2 | — |
| Tower, fortified | 25 gp | 2 | — |
| Trading post | 10 gp | 4 | 2 |

> *OCR caveat:* this table is heavily garbled in the source dump. The cost/day values (20 gp, 5 sp, 5 gp, 10 gp, 4 gp, 50 gp, 400 gp, 2 gp, 25 gp, 1 gp) are confirmed against the visible fragments; some skilled/untrained hireling counts could not be confidently read from the dump and follow the published DMG p.127 table. **Verify against a clean copy of the DMG before relying on the hireling columns.**

### Businesses and garrisons (DMG p.127)

- **Businesses.** An adventurer-owned business can earn enough to cover its own maintenance, but the owner must periodically tend to it (see *Running a Business* below).
- **Garrisons.** Castles and keeps employ **veteran** and **guard** soldiers (Monster Manual); inns, outposts, forts, palaces, and temples rely on **guards**. These warriors make up the bulk of a property's *skilled* hirelings.

*Full detail: DMG p.126–127.*

## Downtime activities (DMG p.127–131)

Letting days, weeks, or months pass between adventures stretches the campaign, paces level progression, and deepens players' investment in the world. As the party levels up, lengthen the downtime so they can pursue bigger projects (a stronghold might be measured in months or years).

> **What lives where.** The *basic* five downtime activities — **Crafting, Practicing a Profession, Recuperating, Researching, Training** — are in the **PHB (Chapter 8, p.187)**; they're summarized first below for reference. The DMG then adds an **optional, expanded set** (DMG p.127–131): Building a Stronghold, Carousing, Crafting a Magic Item, Performing Sacred Rites, Running a Business, Gaining Renown, Selling Magic Items, Sowing Rumors, and the variant Training to Gain Levels. (Note: this is the 2014 DMG's downtime list — it predates and differs from the larger menu in *Xanathar's Guide to Everything*, which adds Buying/Selling Magic Items, Crime, Gambling, Pit Fighting, Scribing a Spell Scroll, Work, and the Bastion-style relationship/complication systems. Those are **not** in this chapter.)

A downtime activity requires a set number of days, and at least **8 hours of each day** must go to the activity for the day to count; the days need not be consecutive (PHB p.187).

### The basic five (PHB p.187 — referenced by the DMG)

| Activity | Time | Cost / resources | Check | Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Crafting** (mundane) | 1 day per 5 gp of market value (e.g., plate armor = 300 days solo) | raw materials worth **half** the item's market value; proficiency with the relevant (usually artisan's) tools, plus any special site (a forge, etc.) | none | Produces a nonmagical item. Multiple proficient crafters can combine (each contributes 5 gp of effort/day). While crafting you maintain a modest lifestyle free, or comfortable at half cost. |
| **Practicing a Profession** | ongoing | — | none | Maintain a **modest** lifestyle without paying the 1 gp/day. Membership in a temple/guild that gives employment → **comfortable**; Performance proficiency put to use → **wealthy**. |
| **Recuperating** | 3 days | — | DC 15 Constitution save | On a success, choose one: end an effect preventing HP recovery, **or** gain advantage for 24 hours on saves vs. one disease/poison affecting you. |
| **Researching** | DM-set | 1 gp/day (atop lifestyle) | DM-set ability checks (e.g., Investigation, Persuasion) | Learn the targeted lore, if the DM rules it available. |
| **Training** (new language or tool) | 250 days | 1 gp/day | DM may require checks; must find a willing instructor | Learn a new language or gain proficiency with a tool. |

### Building a Stronghold (DMG p.128)

A character can use downtime to oversee construction of a stronghold.

- **Prerequisite:** first secure a plot of land — via a **royal charter**, **land grant**, or **deed** (or inheritance). Charters/grants are usually rewards for service but can be bought; deeds can be bought or inherited. A small estate sells for **100–1,000 gp**, a large estate **5,000 gp or more** (if available at all).
- **Construction:** the table gives cost (materials + labor) and time, assuming the character oversees the work. Work continues while the character is away, but **each day away adds 3 days** to the build time.

| Stronghold | Construction Cost | Construction Time |
|---|---:|---:|
| Abbey | 50,000 gp | 400 days |
| Guildhall, town or city | 5,000 gp | 60 days |
| Keep or small castle | 50,000 gp | 400 days |
| Noble estate with manor | 25,000 gp | 150 days |
| Outpost or fort | 15,000 gp | 100 days |
| Palace or large castle | 500,000 gp | 1,200 days |
| Temple | 50,000 gp | 400 days |
| Tower, fortified | 15,000 gp | 100 days |
| Trading post | 5,000 gp | 60 days |

### Carousing (DMG p.128)

Hedonistic downtime — parties, drinking, gambling. The character spends money as if maintaining a **wealthy** lifestyle. At the end, roll **percentile dice + character level** and consult the table (or the DM picks).

| d100 + level | Result |
|---|---|
| 01–10 | You are **jailed** (1d4 days) at the end of the downtime on charges of disorderly conduct and disturbing the peace. Pay a **10 gp fine** to avoid jail, or try to resist arrest. |
| 11–20 | You wake in a strange place with no memory of how you got there, **robbed of 3d6 × 5 gp**. |
| 21–30 | You **make an enemy** (a person, business, or organization, now hostile; DM sets the offended party, you decide how you offended them). |
| 31–40 | A **whirlwind romance** (roll d20: 1–5 ends badly — possible new flaw; 6–10 ends amicably; 11–20 ongoing — the love interest may become a new bond). |
| 41–80 | **Modest gambling winnings**: you recuperate your lifestyle expenses for the time spent carousing. |
| 81–90 | Modest gambling winnings: recuperate your lifestyle expenses **and gain 1d20 × 4 gp**. |
| 91+ | A **small fortune** gambling: recuperate your lifestyle expenses, **gain 4d6 × 10 gp**, and your carousing becomes the stuff of local legend. |

> *OCR caveat:* the Carousing table is scrambled in the dump. The outcomes and dice (3d6×5, 1d20×4, 4d6×10, 10 gp fine, d20 romance bands) match the published DMG p.128; the **"1d4 days" jail duration** for the 01–10 result is the published value but was not legible in the dump — verify if it matters.

### Crafting a Magic Item (DMG p.128–129) — optional rule

Magic items are normally the DM's purview; as an *option*, you may let PCs craft them. Requirements:

- A **formula** describing the item's construction.
- The crafter must be a **spellcaster with spell slots** and able to **cast any spell the item produces**.
- The crafter must meet the **minimum level** for the item's rarity (below). *Example: a 3rd-level character can make a* wand of magic missiles *(uncommon) if they have slots and can cast* magic missile*; the same character could make a +1 weapon — another uncommon item — with no particular spell.*

| Item Rarity | Creation Cost | Minimum Level |
|---|---:|---:|
| Common | 100 gp | 3rd |
| Uncommon | 500 gp | 3rd |
| Rare | 5,000 gp | 6th |
| Very rare | 50,000 gp | 11th |
| Legendary | 500,000 gp | 17th |

- **Pace & cost.** Work proceeds in **25 gp increments per day** (8 hours/day) until the creation cost is paid. So an uncommon item = **20 days and 500 gp**. The DM may freely adjust costs.
- **Spell upkeep.** If the item produces a spell, the creator must expend **one spell slot of that spell's level each day** of creation, and have the spell's material components on hand throughout. Components the spell normally consumes are consumed — once total for a single-use item like a spell scroll, otherwise **once per day** of creation.
- **Collaboration.** Multiple crafters who each meet the level prerequisite can combine efforts; each contributes spells, slots, components, and **25 gp of effort per day** while participating the whole time.
- **Lifestyle.** While crafting, the character maintains a **modest** lifestyle free, or **comfortable at half cost**.
- The DM may also require special materials/locations (alchemist's supplies for a potion, lava to forge a *flame tongue*, etc.).

### Performing Sacred Rites (DMG p.129)

A pious character spends downtime at a temple of a revered god — leading rites (weddings, funerals, ordinations) if a priest there, or making sacrifices / assisting a priest if a layperson. After **at least 10 days**, the character gains **inspiration at the start of each day for the next 2d6 days**.

### Running a Business (DMG p.129)

Maintaining an owned business (an inherited smithy, a gifted tavern or farm). Roll **percentile dice + the number of days spent** (max 30 days added) and consult the table. If a result requires a payment the character can't make, the business begins to fail: each unpaid debt imposes a **−10 penalty** to subsequent rolls on this table.

| d100 + days | Result |
|---|---|
| 01–20 | Pay **1½× the business's maintenance cost** for each of the days. |
| 21–30 | Pay the **full maintenance cost** for each of the days. |
| 31–40 | Pay **half the maintenance cost** for each of the days (profits cover the other half). |
| 41–60 | The business **covers its own maintenance** for each of the days. |
| 61–80 | Covers its own maintenance, **and earns 1d6 × 5 gp profit**. |
| 81–90 | Covers its own maintenance, **and earns 2d8 × 5 gp profit**. |
| 91+ | Covers its own maintenance, **and earns 3d10 × 5 gp profit**. |

### Gaining Renown (DMG p.129)

A character improves **renown** within an organization (see DMG Chapter 1, "Renown") by undertaking minor tasks and socializing with members. After a combined number of days equal to **(current renown × 10)**, the character's **renown increases by 1**.

### Selling a Magic Item (DMG p.129–130)

Done only in a **city** or other place with wealthy buyers. **Legendary items and artifacts can't be sold** during downtime (finding a buyer is its own quest). For each salable common/uncommon/rare/very rare item:

1. Make a **DC 20 Intelligence (Investigation)** check to find a buyer (another character can assist with downtime → advantage). On a failure, **no buyer is found after a 10-day search**. On a success, a buyer is found after a number of days set by rarity (table). Searches for multiple items run **simultaneously** — results aren't added; the whole search lasts as long as the slowest item (so a found common in 2 days + uncommon in 5 days + a failed rare = 10 days total).
2. To set the price, roll **percentile dice + a Charisma (Persuasion) check + the rarity modifier** and read the *Selling a Magic Item* table.

**Salable Magic Items (DMG p.130):**

| Rarity | Base Price | Days to Find Buyer | d100 Roll Modifier* |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Common | 100 gp | 1d4 | +10 |
| Uncommon | 500 gp | 1d6 | +0 |
| Rare | 5,000 gp | 1d8 | −10 |
| Very rare | 50,000 gp | 1d10 | −20 |

\*Applied to the *Selling a Magic Item* roll below.

**Selling a Magic Item (DMG p.130):**

| d100 + mod | You find... |
|---|---|
| 20 or lower | A buyer offering **a tenth** of the base price. |
| 21–40 | A buyer offering **a quarter** of base price, and a shady buyer offering **half**. |
| 41–80 | A buyer offering **half** base price, and a shady buyer offering the **full** base price. |
| 81–90 | A buyer offering the **full** base price. |
| 91+ | A shady buyer offering **1½× the base price**, no questions asked. |

The DM sets the buyer's identity; shady buyers may create later legal complications at the DM's discretion.

### Sowing Rumors (DMG p.131)

Swaying public opinion to elevate a friend or embroil someone in scandal. A rumor must be simple, concrete, hard to disprove, and believable. Time required depends on settlement size, and in a town or city **the time must be continuous** (disappear on an adventure mid-effort and the rumor fades).

| Settlement Size | Time Required |
|---|---|
| Village | 2d6 days |
| Town | 4d6 days |
| City | 6d6 days |

Cost is **1 gp/day** (drinks, social appearances). At the end, make a **DC 15 Charisma (Deception or Persuasion)** check: on a success, the community's general attitude toward the subject shifts **one step** toward friendly or hostile (your choice); on a failure, the rumor gains no traction and further attempts fail. The shift affects the community at large, not every individual.

### Training to Gain Levels (DMG p.131) — variant rule

Optionally, a character who has earned enough XP for a new level must **train before gaining the new level's class features**. Training cost is for the **total** time.

| Level Attained | Training Time | Training Cost |
|---|---:|---:|
| 2nd–4th | 10 days | 20 gp |
| 5th–10th | 20 days | 40 gp |
| 11th–16th | 30 days | 60 gp |
| 17th–20th | 40 days | 80 gp |

### Creating new downtime activities (DMG p.131)

If you invent your own: an activity should **never replace the urge to adventure**; activities with **monetary costs** give players a reason to spend treasure; activities can **reveal hooks and lore** to foreshadow future events; for repeatable activities, build a **random-outcome table** modeled on those in this chapter; and consider granting a **bonus** to a character's checks when their class, proficiency, or background suits the activity.

*Full detail: DMG p.127–131.*

## Common questions

**Q: Where do the basic downtime activities (crafting, profession, recuperating, research, training) live — PHB or DMG?**
A: The PHB (Chapter 8, p.187). The DMG references them and *adds* the expanded optional set (DMG p.127). When in doubt, the PHB is the home of the basic five.

**Q: How often is property maintenance paid?**
A: Every 30 days — 30 × the listed per-day cost. A steward on staff can pay it while the party is away (DMG p.127).

**Q: How long and how much to craft a magic item?**
A: 25 gp of progress per day until the rarity's creation cost is met (Common 100 gp / Uncommon 500 / Rare 5,000 / Very rare 50,000 / Legendary 500,000), so e.g. an uncommon item is 20 days and 500 gp. You must also meet the minimum level (3rd/3rd/6th/11th/17th), have the formula, be a caster able to cast any spell the item makes, and spend a spell slot per day if it produces a spell (DMG p.128–129).

**Q: Can a PC just sell a legendary item or artifact in town?**
A: No — the Selling a Magic Item downtime activity covers only common through very rare. Offloading a legendary item or artifact is meant to be an adventure in itself (DMG p.130).

**Q: Does carousing make money?**
A: Sometimes. You pay for a wealthy lifestyle up front; rolls of 41+ recuperate that cost and rolls of 81+ add winnings (1d20×4 gp, or 4d6×10 gp at 91+). Low rolls cost you — jail, robbery (3d6×5 gp), or an enemy (DMG p.128).

**Q: Are Crime, Gambling, Pit Fighting, Scribing a Spell Scroll, and Work in this chapter?**
A: No. Those are part of the expanded downtime system in *Xanathar's Guide to Everything*, not the 2014 DMG. This chapter's optional set is the nine listed above (stronghold, carousing, magic-item crafting, sacred rites, business, renown, selling magic items, sowing rumors, level-training).
