# Treasure and Magic Items

How treasure works at the table: the random-treasure system (individual treasure vs. hoards, organized by CR tier), coin/gem/art-object values, and the full set of **magic-item rules** a DM actually reaches for — rarity, attunement, identifying, activating, charges, potions, scrolls, sentient items, and artifacts. The back half is an **index** of every magic item in the chapter (name → rarity → attunement → printed page) so you can jump straight to the PDF for the full text.

**Source:** DMG Chapter 7, "Treasure" (printed pages 133–234). DMG printed page = physical PDF page (offset 0).

> **Scope note:** This file covers the *rules* and provides an *index*. It does **not** reproduce individual magic-item, artifact, or "Other Rewards" descriptions (copyright + length). For the actual item text, go to the cited DMG page or `.build/dmg.txt`.

## Part 1 — Treasure rules

### Types of treasure (DMG p.133)

- **Coins** — cp, sp, ep, gp, pp. **50 coins of any type weigh 1 pound.**
- **Gemstones** — small, lightweight, easily secured (see values below).
- **Art objects** — idols, necklaces, paintings, bejeweled dishes (see values below).
- **Magic items** — armor, potions, scrolls, rings, rods, staffs, wands, weapons, wondrous items; each has a rarity (common → legendary). Intelligent monsters often use the items they own; others stash them away.

### Random treasure — the method (DMG p.133)

Treasure is allocated by a monster's **challenge rating**, using four tiers of tables: **CR 0–4, CR 5–10, CR 11–16, and CR 17+**. There are two kinds of table:

- **Individual Treasure tables (p.136)** — what *one* creature carries on its person ("pocket change"). Roll d100 on the table matching the monster's CR; read across for coins of each type (each entry also lists an *average* in parentheses so you can skip the roll). For a group of similar creatures, roll once and multiply by the number in the group. If coins don't fit a monster, convert to gems/art of equal value.
- **Treasure Hoard tables (p.137–139)** — a large cache: the wealth of a group (use the *leader's* CR), of a single hoarder like a dragon (its own CR), of an unowned hoard (the CR presiding over the lair), or a benefactor's reward (use the party's average level). Every hoard rolls a fixed block of coins (shown atop each table), then a single d100 roll determines gems/art **and** any magic items together. **Legendary hoarders: always roll at least twice and add the results.**

> **Whole-campaign benchmark (DMG p.133):** over a typical campaign a party finds hoards totaling **7 rolls** on the Challenge 0–4 table, **18 rolls** on Challenge 5–10, **12 rolls** on Challenge 11–16, and **8 rolls** on Challenge 17+. Placement is entirely at the DM's discretion.

*(The big coin/gem/hoard d100 tables themselves are not reproduced here — see DMG p.136–139.)*

### Magic items in hoards: Tables A–I (DMG p.137–139, 144–149)

When a hoard roll indicates magic items, you roll on lettered tables **A–I** (DMG p.144–149). Roughly, lower letters = lower rarity/lower tier: **A–B** (common/uncommon, low tiers) up through **H–I** (very rare/legendary, high tiers). The hoard tables tell you which lettered table to roll on and how many times.

### Mundane rewards & buying/selling (DMG p.135)

- **Magic-item value by rarity** is set by the DM using the rarity table below. **A consumable** (potion, scroll) is typically **half** the value of a permanent item of the same rarity.
- **Most magic items aren't for sale** — they're too rare, and the main obstacle to *selling* one is finding a buyer. Common items (e.g., *potion of healing*) can sometimes be procured from an alchemist, herbalist, or spellcaster, often for a service rather than coin. Larger trade markets are a DM option (City of Brass, Sigil, etc.), usually invitation-only and crawling with thieves. Selling is handled via the downtime rules in DMG ch.6.

## Part 2 — Magic item rules (the at-the-table part)

### Rarity (DMG p.135)

Every magic item is **common, uncommon, rare, very rare, or legendary.** Rarity is a rough measure of power and a soft gate on when items appear — but story trumps it ("if you want a *ring of invisibility* in a 1st-level character's hands, so be it").

| Rarity | Character level (suggested) | Value |
|---|---|---|
| Common | 1st or higher | 50–100 gp |
| Uncommon | 1st or higher | 101–500 gp |
| Rare | 5th or higher | 501–5,000 gp |
| Very rare | 11th or higher | 5,001–50,000 gp |
| Legendary | 17th or higher | 50,001+ gp |

### Identifying a magic item (DMG p.136)

Handling an item tells you it's magical, but **not** what it does. Two ways to learn its properties:

- The **identify spell** (fastest), or
- Focusing on **one item during a short rest** while in physical contact with it; at the rest's end you learn its properties and how to use them.

**Potions are the exception** — a small taste reveals what a potion does. Items can also carry clues (a command word etched inside a ring; a feather motif suggesting *feather falling*). **Variant — More Difficult Identification (p.136):** remove short-rest identification and require *identify*, experimentation, or both.

### Attunement (DMG p.136, 138)

Some items must be **attuned** before their magical properties work; without attunement you get only the item's nonmagical benefits (e.g., a magic shield still works as a normal shield).

- **How:** spend a **short rest focused on that one item** while touching it (can't be the same short rest used to identify it). Interrupting the rest fails the attempt.
- **Prerequisites:** an item may require a class. A **spellcasting class** prerequisite is met by a monster that has spell slots and uses that class's spell list.
- **The limit: a creature can be attuned to no more than 3 items at a time.** A fourth attunement fails until you end one. You can't attune to **more than one copy** of the same item.
- **An item attunes to only one creature at a time.**
- **Attunement ends** if: you no longer meet the prerequisites; the item is **more than 100 feet away for 24+ hours**; you die; or another creature attunes to it. You can also **end it voluntarily** with another short rest focused on the item — **unless it's cursed** (a cursed item's attunement can't be ended voluntarily until the curse is broken, e.g., *remove curse*).

### Cursed items (DMG p.138–139)

A magic item's description states whether it's cursed. Most identification methods (including *identify*) **fail to reveal a curse** — it should be a surprise sprung when its effects show. (See, e.g., *armor of vulnerability*, *berserker axe*, *shield of missile attraction*, *sword of vengeance* in the index below.)

### Wearing & wielding items (DMG p.140–141)

- A worn item must be **donned in the intended way** (boots on feet, ring on finger, weapon in hand). Most worn items auto-fit any size/build; rare items may be made to fit only certain creatures (drow armor for elves, etc.).
- **Nonhumanoids:** DM's discretion whether the item works (a ring on a tentacle might; boots on a snake-tailed yuan-ti won't).
- **Multiple of the same kind:** common sense — normally only one pair of footwear, one pair of gloves/gauntlets, one suit of armor, one headwear, one cloak (exceptions allowed).
- **Paired items** (boots, bracers, gauntlets, gloves) only work if **both** of the matching pair are worn.

### Item resilience (DMG p.141)

A magic item is at least as durable as its nonmagical version. **Most magic items (other than potions and scrolls) have resistance to all damage.** Artifacts are practically indestructible. (An Ioun stone, for reference, is treated as a worn object with **AC 24, 10 HP, resistance to all damage.**)

### Activating an item (DMG p.141)

- An activation that **requires an action is *not* a Use an Item action** — so a rogue's Fast Hands can't trigger it.
- **Command word:** a spoken word/phrase; can't be used in an area of magical *silence*.
- **Consumables:** used up on activation (potion swallowed, oil applied, scroll's writing vanishes).
- **Spells from items (p.142):** cast at the **lowest possible level**, expend **no spell slot**, need **no components** unless stated; normal casting time/range/duration; concentrate if the spell requires it. If an item uses *your* spellcasting ability and you have none (e.g., a rogue using Use Magic Device), your spellcasting **modifier is +0** but **your proficiency bonus does apply**.
- **Charges (p.142):** some items have charges that must be expended. An *identify* spell — and attuning — reveals the current number; when an item recharges, the attuned creature learns how many it regained. (Many recharge at dawn; expending the *last* charge of a wand/staff often risks destruction on a d20 roll of 1.)
- **Variant — Wands that don't recharge (p.141):** make a wand a finite resource (up to 25 charges, never regained).
- **Magic item formulas (p.141–142):** an optional reward — a formula is **one step rarer** than the item it makes; no formulas exist for legendary items.

### Magic item categories (DMG p.139–140)

Armor (must be worn to function) · Potions (consumable) · Rings (worn on a finger) · Rods · Scrolls (consumable) · Staffs (usable as a quarterstaff unless stated) · Wands · Weapons · Wondrous items (the catch-all: worn gear, bags, figurines, etc.).

### Potions (DMG p.139–140)

- Drinking a potion, or administering one to someone else, **takes an action**; an oil may take longer. Effect is immediate; the potion is used up.
- ***Potion of healing* family (p.188):** Healing (common) **2d4+2** · Greater (uncommon) **4d4+4** · Superior (rare) **8d4+8** · Supreme (very rare) **10d4+20**.
- **Variant — Mixing Potions (p.140):** drinking/combining potions can interact unpredictably; roll on the **Potion Miscibility** table (d100: results range from a 6d10-force explosion, to no effect, to halved/doubled effects, to one effect becoming permanent).

### Scrolls & spell scrolls (DMG p.139, 200)

A scroll is a **consumable**; reading it takes an action, after which it's used up. Any creature that can read the script can attempt to activate it.

**Spell scroll (DMG p.200):**
- If the spell is **on your class list**, read it as an action and cast it with **no components**.
- If it's on your list but **above your level**, make a spellcasting-ability check, **DC 10 + spell level**; on a fail, the spell is lost with no effect.
- A wizard can **copy** a wizard spell from a scroll into a spellbook with an Intelligence (Arcana) check, **DC 10 + spell level**; success or fail, the scroll is destroyed.

### Wands & staffs — general charges (DMG p.140, 142)

- A **wand** is ~15 in. long; a **staff** ~5–6 ft and usable as a quarterstaff unless stated.
- Both commonly run on **charges** that recharge (often **at dawn**). Many wands/staffs make you roll a **d20 when you expend the last charge**: on a **1** the item is typically destroyed (crumbles to ash, etc.); some staffs add a "**20** = regains charges" result. Specific recharge dice are per-item (see index pages).

### Sentient magic items (DMG p.214–216)

Some items are sentient — possessed, haunted, or self-aware. They function as **NPCs under the DM's control**; **activated properties belong to the item, not the wielder.** A cooperative item lets the wielder use them; a strained relationship lets the item **suppress or even turn them against** the wielder (a *conflict*, often resolved by a contest). **Consumables (potions, scrolls) are never sentient.**

When creating one (p.214–216): give it **Int/Wis/Cha scores**, a means of **communication** (emotion, speech, or telepathy), **senses** (hearing + vision/darkvision out to 30–120 ft), an **alignment**, NPC-style personality, and optionally a **special purpose** it pursues. Sample sentient items in the chapter: ***Blackrazor*** (p.216), ***Moonblade*** (p.217), ***Wave*** (p.218), ***Whelm*** (p.218).

### Artifacts (DMG p.219–227)

An **artifact** is a unique item of tremendous power and history — a plot device as much as a magic item; they appear only when the DM wants them. Each has its own potent properties plus optional **minor/major beneficial and detrimental properties** (up to 4 minor + 2 major of each kind, p.219–221), which can change each time the artifact resurfaces. Artifacts are practically indestructible — each has a specific, dramatic means of destruction.

## Common questions

**Q: How many magic items can a character attune to?**
A: **Three** at a time. A fourth attunement fails until you end one (DMG p.138).

**Q: Does an item I'm not attuned to do anything?**
A: Only its **nonmagical** benefits (a magic shield still gives a shield's AC), unless the description says otherwise (DMG p.138).

**Q: How do I identify an item, and how long does it take?**
A: A **short rest** focused on the one item (or the *identify* spell). Potions are identified by a taste (DMG p.136).

**Q: What are a spell scroll's save DC and attack bonus?**
A: By spell level — e.g., cantrip/1st = DC 13/+5; up to 9th = DC 19/+11 (full table at DMG p.200). Casting a scroll above your level needs a **DC 10 + spell level** check (DMG p.200).

**Q: A player wants to buy a magic item — can they?**
A: Usually not; most items aren't for sale, and rarity sets value (consumables = half). Common items can sometimes be sourced from a specialist, often for a service (DMG p.135).

**Q: How much does treasure scale with CR?**
A: Use the table tier matching the monster's CR (0–4 / 5–10 / 11–16 / 17+). Individual tables = pocket change; hoard tables = big caches (DMG p.133–139).

**Q: Can a potion or scroll be sentient?**
A: No — consumables are never sentient (DMG p.214).
