# Running the Game

The DM's at-the-table toolkit: when to call for a roll and how to set the DC, how ability checks differ from saves and attack rolls, a structured way to run social interaction, the chase rules, and the mechanics for diseases, poisons, and madness. This file focuses on the rulings you actually reach for mid-session; table-management advice (table rules, missing players, metagame thinking) and other long subsections are pointed to rather than reproduced.

**Source:** DMG Chapter 8, "Running the Game" (printed pages 235–262).

> **Quick lookup for DCs:** [`../quick-reference/difficulty-classes.md`](../quick-reference/difficulty-classes.md) has the Typical DCs and advantage/disadvantage in cheat-sheet form. ## Table management (pointer) (DMG p.235–236)

The chapter opens with practical group advice — table rules, table talk, handling **missing players** (have someone else run them, run them yourself, write them out, or fade them into the background), **small groups** (let players run extra characters or add NPC followers), **new players**, **rolling in the open vs. behind a screen** (a screen lets you fudge or fake rolls to preserve mystery — use sparingly), and **metagame thinking**. This is good first-campaign reading but isn't rules you look up mid-fight. *Full detail: DMG p.235–236.*

## When to call for a roll (DMG p.237)

The default is **no roll**. Let an attempt simply succeed when there's no meaningful consequence for failure — a character doesn't roll Dexterity to walk across an empty room or Charisma to order a mug of ale. Before reaching for the d20, ask two questions (DMG p.237):

1. **Is the task so easy and free of stress that there should be no chance of failure?**
2. **Is the task so inappropriate or impossible** (e.g., hitting the moon with an arrow) **that it can't work?**

If the answer to both is *no*, some kind of roll is appropriate. The next sections sort that roll into an ability check, an attack roll, or a saving throw.

The DMG frames three styles for how much to lean on dice (DMG p.236–237): **rolling with it** (roll for nearly everything; be ready to improvise), **ignoring the dice** (decide outcomes from how players describe and justify their actions), and **the middle path** (most DMs blend the two). Remember: *dice don't run your game — you do.* You can always declare an action an automatic success, or grant advantage to soften a bad roll.

## Ability checks, contests, attack rolls, and saving throws (DMG p.237–238)

- **Ability check** — a test of a task a character *actively chooses* to attempt. (DMG p.237)
- **Contest** — an ability check matched against another creature's check rather than a fixed DC; use it when one creature directly opposes another (hiding = Dexterity vs. Wisdom; arm-wrestling or holding a door = Strength vs. Strength). (DMG p.238)
- **Attack roll** — call for one when a character tries to *hit* a creature or object, especially when armor, a shield, or cover could foil it. Works for non-combat too (archery contests, darts). (DMG p.238)
- **Saving throw** — an *instant, almost-never-chosen* response to a harmful effect. The effect (spell, monster ability, trap) usually names the save and DC; when a situation clearly calls for one but no rule provides it, you pick the ability. (DMG p.238)

The DMG's **Saving Throws** guide (DMG p.238): **Str** resists being physically moved or bound; **Dex** dodges out of harm's way; **Con** endures disease, poison, or vitality-sapping hazards; **Int** disbelieves illusions and resists logic-refutable mental assaults; **Wis** resists charm, fear, and willpower attacks; **Cha** withstands possession or being hurled to another plane.

**Int check vs. Wis check** (a common ruling): **Wisdom** is *perceiving* what's around you (noticing a clean patch of wall); **Intelligence** is *interpreting why* (deducing the clean patch means a secret door). (DMG p.238)

### Multiple ability checks / retries (DMG p.237)
Sometimes a character can just keep trying — the only cost is time. Rule of thumb: a character spending **ten times the normal time** on a task **automatically succeeds**. But no number of retries turns an *impossible* task into a success. In other cases, a failure makes a repeat of the *same* check pointless (the same lie won't work on the same guard twice) — the players must find a new approach.

*Full detail: DMG p.237–238.*

## Difficulty Classes (DMG p.238)

When a rule or adventure doesn't hand you a DC, set one by gauging how hard the task is and reading off the table.

### Typical DCs (DMG p.238)

| Task difficulty | DC |
|---|---:|
| Very easy | 5 |
| Easy | 10 |
| Moderate | 15 |
| Hard | 20 |
| Very hard | 25 |
| Nearly impossible | 30 |

Practical guidance (DMG p.238):

- If you've decided a check is warranted, the task usually isn't *very easy* — most people clear a DC 5 task without rolling, so just let them.
- **You can run the whole game on 10 / 15 / 20.** A character with a 10 in the ability and no proficiency clears an easy (DC 10) task about **50%** of the time. Moderate usually needs a good score *or* proficiency; hard usually needs *both* (plus luck).
- Use DC 25+ with caution and mind the party's level: DC 25 is *very hard* for low-level PCs but reasonable past ~10th level; DC 30 is *nearly impossible* for low-level PCs — even a 20th-level character with proficiency and a 20 in the ability needs a **19 or 20** on the die.

### Proficiency (DMG p.239)
When you ask for a check, consider whether a **skill or tool proficiency** applies — and let players make the case. Test: *could a character get better at this through training and practice?* If yes, an appropriate proficiency applies. You can also let a skill ride a *different* ability than usual (e.g., a long swim becomes a **Constitution (Athletics)** check instead of Strength). Tool proficiency adds the bonus to checks that *use* the tool, not to checks that merely involve the same subject (a carpenter's-tools proficiency helps craft a flute but not spot unsafe construction). Save and attack proficiency is all-or-nothing — the bonus always applies if you're proficient.

### Advantage and disadvantage (DMG p.239–240)
These model temporary circumstances and are also a fine reward for clever play. Grant **advantage** when an outside edge, a helpful environment, exceptional creativity, or a prior helpful action improves the odds; impose **disadvantage** when circumstances, the environment, or a poor plan hurt them. **They don't stack and they cancel:** any amount of advantage plus any amount of disadvantage = a straight roll, and you stop counting factors. (Worked example: a wizard running past an ambush has disadvantage for not paying attention but advantage from the ogres' noisy winch — net neither.)

> **Optional — automatic success (DMG p.239):** a character auto-succeeds on any *ability check* whose DC ≤ (relevant ability score − 5); not used for contests, saves, or attacks. Optionally, a proficient character auto-succeeds at DC 10 or less (DC 15 or less at 11th level+). Downside: predictability.

*Full detail: DMG p.238–240.*

## Inspiration (pointer) (DMG p.240–241)

Inspiration grants advantage on one check, attack, or save (max one held at a time). The DMG suggests awarding it about **once per session per character**, for roleplaying, heroic risk-taking, victories, or genre emulation — and notes variants (spend after a roll; players award it to each other; or ignore it entirely). Mostly a campaign-flavor lever, not a mid-session lookup. *Full detail: DMG p.240–241.*

## Resolution and consequences (DMG p.242)

Outcomes don't have to be binary. Useful tools when adjudicating success and failure:

- **Success at a cost (DMG p.242).** When a character misses the DC by **1 or 2** (or hits it exactly), you can grant success but bolt on a complication that *changes the situation* — the hit lands but the PC is disarmed; the climb succeeds but the rope below is fraying; the intimidation works but the kobold shrieks and alerts others.
- **Degrees of failure (DMG p.242).** Let the *margin* matter: failing a trap-disarm by **5 or more** springs the trap, a lesser failure just doesn't trigger it; failing a Persuasion check loses the queen's help, failing by 5+ lands you in her dungeon.
- **Critical success/failure on checks and saves (DMG p.242).** A natural 20 or 1 on an *ability check or saving throw* normally means nothing, but you may amplify the outcome — a natural 1 picking a lock might snap the thieves' tools; a natural 20 on Investigation might surface an extra clue. (This is optional; only attack rolls crit by the core rules.)

## Social interaction (DMG p.244–246)

A structured way to resolve a social scene. The structure runs *underneath* the roleplaying — players never need to see the gears — and it's not a replacement for talking it out. Four steps:

### 1. Starting attitude (DMG p.244)
Pick the NPC's attitude toward the party:

- **Friendly** — wants the party to succeed. Helps freely on anything with no real risk, effort, or cost; a successful Charisma check is needed only to get a friendly creature to take on *personal risk*.
- **Indifferent** — may help or hinder based on self-interest (can still be polite or surly). A **successful Charisma check is needed** to persuade it to do anything.
- **Hostile** — opposes the party's goals (but doesn't necessarily attack on sight — think a scheming noble). The party needs **one or more challenging Charisma checks** to get any cooperation. A truly ill-disposed NPC may be beyond persuasion, in which case diplomacy auto-fails.

### 2. Conversation (DMG p.244–245)
Play it out. Attitude can shift **at most one step** during a single interaction (temporarily or permanently): saying the right things (touching an NPC's **ideal, bond, or flaw**) can warm hostile → indifferent or indifferent → friendly; a gaffe, insult, or harmful act can cool friendly → indifferent or indifferent → hostile. To target those characteristics deliberately, the party first has to *learn* them — a **Wisdom (Insight)** check (DC set by you) can reveal one; a check that **fails by 10 or more** lets you feed them a *false* characteristic.

### 3. Charisma check (DMG p.245)
At the request/demand, or when the scene has run its course, call for one **Charisma (Persuasion / Deception / Intimidation)** check from any character who actively participated. The NPC's current attitude sets the DC needed for a given reaction:

**Conversation Reaction (DMG p.245)**

| DC | Friendly creature | Indifferent creature | Hostile creature |
|---:|---|---|---|
| **0**  | Does as asked, taking no risk or sacrifice | Offers no help but does no harm | Opposes the party and might take risks to do so |
| **10** | Accepts a *minor* risk or sacrifice to do as asked | Does as asked if no risk or sacrifice is involved | Offers no help but does no harm |
| **20** | Accepts a *significant* risk or sacrifice to do as asked | Accepts a *minor* risk or sacrifice to do as asked | Does as asked if no risk or sacrifice is involved |

(Read it as a sliding scale: the friendlier the NPC, the more a given check buys you; a hostile NPC is effectively two steps "behind" a friendly one.)

**Aiding the check (DMG p.245).** A second character who meaningfully contributes grants **advantage** on the check; an inadvertent insult or counterproductive remark imposes **disadvantage**. Some scenes (multiple goals) call for multiple checks.

### 4. Repeat? (DMG p.245)
After a check, pushing again is often fruitless and risks *worsening* the NPC's attitude toward hostility. Use judgment — and a different character may be able to defuse a botched exchange with clever roleplay and a fresh Persuasion check.

The chapter also covers **roleplaying NPCs** (using your voice, face, and arms; engaging quieter players) — pointer only. *Full detail: DMG p.244–246.*

## Chases (DMG p.252–255)

Strict movement rules make chases dull (faster always catches slower; equal speeds never close). These rules add randomness and tension.

**Setup (DMG p.252).** A chase needs a quarry and ≥1 pursuer; anyone not already in initiative rolls it. Each participant takes **one action and a move** per turn. Track the distance between sides; the pursuer nearest the quarry is the **lead** (which can change round to round).

**Dashing (DMG p.252).** Everyone is motivated to **Dash** every round. A participant can Dash freely **a number of times equal to 3 + its Constitution modifier**. Each Dash *beyond* that requires a **DC 10 Constitution check at the end of the turn** or it gains a **level of exhaustion**. A participant drops out at **exhaustion level 5** (speed 0). Exhaustion from the chase is cleared by a short or long rest.

**Attacks (DMG p.252–253).** Chase participants can attack and cast spells normally, but **can't make opportunity attacks against each other** (everyone's moving the same way). Creatures *not* in the chase (e.g., thugs they run past) can still OA them.

**Ending it (DMG p.253).** When neither side quits, the **quarry makes a Dexterity (Stealth) check at the end of each round** vs. the pursuers' **passive Wisdom (Perception)**. If the quarry was *never out of the lead pursuer's sight*, the check **auto-fails**. Otherwise, beat the highest passive Perception to escape; if not, the chase continues. The **Escape Factors** table grants advantage (lots to hide behind; very crowded/noisy area) or disadvantage (few hiding spots; uncrowded/quiet area; lead pursuer is a ranger or has Survival proficiency).

**Complications (DMG p.253–254).** At the end of each turn, a participant rolls a **d20** on the **Urban** or **Wilderness Chase Complications** table; on **1–10** a complication hits the *next* participant in initiative order (**11–20** = no complication), and inspiration can negate it. These are full d20 tables of obstacles (crowds, slippery ground, snares, stampedes, razorvine, etc., each with its own DC and effect) — not reproduced here; roll on the tables at DMG p.254. The section closes with notes on designing your own tables, splitting up, mapping the chase, and role reversal. *Full detail: DMG p.252–255.*

## Diseases (DMG p.256–257)

A disease is primarily a **plot device** — the rules describe effects and cures, but there's no universal disease subsystem, and you should freely adjust DCs, incubation times, and symptoms to fit your story. A trivial bug is just a resource drain (cured by *lesser restoration*); a serious plague can anchor a whole adventure. The DMG gives three samples (numbers exact, effects paraphrased):

### Cackle Fever ("the shrieks") (DMG p.257)
Targets humanoids; **gnomes are immune**. Symptoms appear **1d4 hours** after infection (fever, disorientation) and the victim gains **one level of exhaustion** that can't be removed until cured.
- Any great stress (entering combat, taking damage, fear, a nightmare) forces a **DC 13 Constitution save**. On a fail: **5 (1d10) psychic damage** and **incapacitated with mad laughter for 1 minute** (repeat the save at the end of each turn to end it).
- Any humanoid starting its turn within **10 ft** of a victim mid-laughter must make a **DC 10 Con save** or also catch the disease (success grants 24-hour immunity to *that* victim's laughter).
- At the end of each long rest, a **DC 13 Con save**; each success lowers both this DC and the mad-laughter DC by **1d6**. When the DC hits **0**, the victim recovers. **Three failed saves** inflict a random **indefinite madness** (see below).

### Sewer Plague (DMG p.257)
A catch-all for filth-borne illnesses (carried by rats, otyughs, etc.). On a bite or contact with contaminated filth, make a **DC 11 Constitution save** or become infected. Symptoms appear in **1d4 days** (fatigue, cramps): **one level of exhaustion**, plus the victim regains only **half** the normal HP from Hit Dice and **no** HP from a long rest.
- At the end of each long rest, a **DC 11 Con save**: failure adds a level of exhaustion, success removes one. Dropping exhaustion below level 1 cures the disease.

### Sight Rot (DMG p.257)
Causes bleeding eyes and eventual blindness. A beast or humanoid drinking tainted water makes a **DC 15 Constitution save** or is infected. One day later, vision blurs: a **−1 penalty to attack rolls and sight-based ability checks**, worsening by **1 at the end of each long rest**; at **−5** the victim is **blinded** until cured by magic (*lesser restoration*, *heal*).
- Curable without magic via the swamp flower **Eyebright**: with an **herbalism kit**, one hour makes one dose of ointment. Applied before a long rest, one dose **halts** worsening for that rest; **three doses** cure it entirely.

*Full detail: DMG p.256–257.*

## Poisons (DMG p.257–258)

Poisons come in **four types** by delivery (DMG p.257):

- **Contact** — affects a creature that touches it with exposed skin.
- **Ingested** — must swallow a full dose (a partial dose may give advantage on the save or half damage, at your discretion).
- **Inhaled** — a powder or gas; **one dose fills a 5-foot cube**.
- **Injury** — affects a creature taking slashing/piercing damage from a coated weapon or ammunition.

All save DCs below are **Constitution** saves; prices are per single dose. Numbers verified against the dump.

| Poison | Type | Price/dose | Save | Effect (paraphrased) |
|---|---|---:|:---:|---|
| **Assassin's Blood** | Ingested | 150 gp | DC 10 | Fail: **6 (1d12)** poison damage + **poisoned 24 hr**. Success: half damage, not poisoned. |
| **Basic Poison** | Injury | 100 gp | — | The poison craftable with a poisoner's kit (PHB). Coats a weapon/ammo; the basic-poison effect is the PHB vial — see PHB equipment. |
| **Burnt Othur Fumes** | Inhaled | 500 gp | DC 13 | Fail: **10 (3d6)** poison damage; repeat save at start of each turn, taking **3 (1d6)** on each further fail. Ends after **3 successes**. |

## Madness (DMG p.258–260)

Optional rules for horror-themed campaigns. Madness can come from certain spells (e.g., *contact other plane*, *symbol*), diseases, poisons, planar effects (psychic wind, Pandemonium's winds), or some artifacts. **Resisting a madness-inducing effect is normally a Wisdom or Charisma save** (or a Sanity save if you use the optional Sanity score from Chapter 9). (DMG p.258–259)

### The three durations (DMG p.259)
- **Short-term** — one effect from the table below for **1d10 minutes**.
- **Long-term** — one effect for **1d10 × 10 hours**.
- **Indefinite** — a new **character flaw** that lasts **until cured**.

### Short-Term Madness (d100) (DMG p.259) — paraphrased
| d100 | Effect (1d10 minutes) |
|---|---|
| 01–20 | Retreats inward and is **paralyzed**; ends if the character takes any damage. |
| 21–30 | **Incapacitated**, spending the duration screaming, laughing, or weeping. |
| 31–40 | **Frightened**; must use action and movement to flee the source of fear. |
| 41–50 | Babbling; **can't speak normally or cast spells**. |
| 51–60 | Must use its action each round to **attack the nearest creature**. |
| 61–70 | Vivid hallucinations; **disadvantage on ability checks**. |
| 71–75 | Does whatever anyone tells them, so long as it isn't obviously self-destructive. |
| 76–80 | Overpowering urge to eat something strange (dirt, slime, offal). |
| 81–90 | **Stunned**. |
| 91–100 | Falls **unconscious**. |

### Long-Term Madness (d100) (DMG p.260) — paraphrased
| d100 | Effect (1d10 × 10 hours) |
|---|---|
| 01–10 | Compelled to repeat an activity (washing hands, counting coins, praying). |
| 11–20 | Vivid hallucinations; **disadvantage on ability checks**. |
| 21–30 | Extreme paranoia; **disadvantage on Wisdom and Charisma checks**. |
| 31–40 | Intense revulsion toward something (usually the madness's source), as the *antipathy* effect of *antipathy/sympathy*. |
| 41–45 | Powerful delusion that they're under the effects of a chosen potion. |
| 46–55 | Fixated on a "lucky charm"; **disadvantage on attacks, checks, and saves while more than 30 ft from it**. |
| 56–65 | **Blinded** (25%) or **deafened** (75%). |
| 66–75 | Uncontrollable tremors/tics; **disadvantage on Strength- and Dexterity-based attacks, checks, and saves**. |
| 76–85 | Partial amnesia; knows self, racial traits, and class features but recognizes no one and recalls nothing before the madness. |
| 86–90 | On taking damage, **DC 15 Wisdom save** or be affected as a failed save vs. *confusion* for 1 minute. |
| 91–95 | **Loses the ability to speak**. |
| 96–100 | Falls **unconscious**; nothing (jostling or damage) wakes them. |

### Indefinite Madness (d100) (DMG p.260) — paraphrased flaws
A persistent character flaw until cured. Examples across the d100 table: "Being drunk keeps me sane" (01–15); "I keep whatever I find" (16–25); imitating someone else's dress, manner, and name (26–30); compulsive lying/exaggeration to stay interesting (31–35); tunnel-vision on a single goal (36–45); apathy toward everything around them (46–50); resentment of being judged (51–55); grandiosity — "I'm the smartest, strongest, etc." (56–70); paranoia about ever-present enemies and watchers (71–80); trusting only an imaginary "special friend" only they can see (81–85); finding everything funny, more so the more serious it gets (86–95); and "I've discovered that I really like killing people" (96–100).

### Curing madness (DMG p.260)
- **calm emotions** — *suppresses* madness's effects (doesn't cure).
- **lesser restoration** — rids a character of **short-term or long-term** madness.
- **remove curse** or **dispel evil** — may work depending on the source.
- **greater restoration** (or more powerful magic) — required to cure **indefinite** madness.

*Full detail: DMG p.258–260.*

## Common questions

**Q: When should I make players roll?**
A: Only when failure has a meaningful consequence *and* the task isn't trivial or impossible. If it's a sure thing or a non-starter, decide it without dice (DMG p.237).

**Q: What DC should I set?**
A: Gauge the difficulty and read the Typical DCs table — you can run almost everything on 10/15/20 (easy/moderate/hard). DC 25+ is for very-hard tasks and scales with party level (DMG p.238).

**Q: Ability check or saving throw?**
A: A check is something a character *actively attempts*; a save is a *reflexive response* to something happening *to* them. If they're deciding to do it, it's a check (DMG p.237–238).

**Q: A player wants to retry a failed check — can they?**
A: If failure just costs time, yes (and ten times the normal time = automatic success). But you can't retry an *impossible* task, and some failures (a blown lie) make the same check pointless until the approach changes (DMG p.237).

**Q: How do I shift an NPC's attitude in a conversation?**
A: Hit their ideal, bond, or flaw — but attitude moves at most **one step** per interaction. To target a flaw deliberately, the party may first need a Wisdom (Insight) check to learn it. The Charisma check's success against the Conversation Reaction table then determines exactly what the NPC will risk (DMG p.244–245).

**Q: Does a natural 20 on a skill check or save do anything special?**
A: Not by default — only attack rolls auto-crit. You *may* optionally amplify a natural 20 or 1 on a check/save (an extra clue, snapped tools), but it's a DM call (DMG p.242).

**Q: How does the chase Dash limit work?**
A: Free Dashes equal **3 + Con modifier**; each extra Dash needs a DC 10 Con check or a level of exhaustion. Hit exhaustion 5 and you're out (DMG p.252).

**Q: How does a quarry escape a chase?**
A: At the end of each round, the quarry rolls Dexterity (Stealth) vs. the pursuers' highest passive Perception — but it auto-fails if it was never out of the lead pursuer's sight (DMG p.253).

**Q: How do I cure madness?**
A: *calm emotions* suppresses it; *lesser restoration* cures short- or long-term madness; *greater restoration* (or stronger magic) is needed for indefinite madness (DMG p.260).
