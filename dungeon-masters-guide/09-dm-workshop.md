# Dungeon Master's Workshop

The DMG's grab-bag of **optional and variant rules** for customizing a campaign — ability-score variants (proficiency dice, Hero Points, Honor, Sanity), rest/healing variants, alternate initiative and combat options (flanking, lingering injuries, cleaving), plus design procedures for homebrewing monsters, spells, magic items, and character options.

**Source:** DMG Chapter 9, "Dungeon Master's Workshop" (printed pages 263–289).

> **Everything in this chapter is OPTIONAL.** None of it is part of default 5e (2014). Don't assume any rule here is in play unless the DM has explicitly adopted it. The DMG advises trying **no more than one or two options at a time** so you can judge each one's effect before adding more (DMG p.263). A rule serves the table, not the other way around — refine or drop anything that isn't improving the game.

> **General caution (DMG p.263):** be wary of anything that lets a character concentrate on more than one effect, take more than one reaction or bonus action per round, or attune to more than three magic items — those break core assumptions.

## Ability-score options (DMG p.263–266)

### Proficiency Dice (optional) — DMG p.263

Replaces a character's flat **proficiency bonus** with a **rolled die**, adding randomness so proficiency is a less reliable indicator of mastery. Instead of adding the bonus to an ability check, attack roll, or saving throw, the player rolls the die below. If a feature (e.g., the rogue's Expertise) would let the character double the proficiency bonus, the player **rolls the proficiency die twice** instead. Intended for PCs and leveled NPCs only — not for monsters.

| Level | Proficiency Bonus | Proficiency Die |
|---|---|---|
| 1st–4th | +2 | 1d4 |
| 5th–8th | +3 | 1d6 |
| 9th–12th | +4 | 1d8 |
| 13th–16th | +5 | 1d10 |
| 17th–20th | +6 | 1d12 |

### Skill variants (optional) — DMG p.263–265

Three ways to dispense with the skill list entirely (pick one):

- **Ability Check Proficiency** — drop skills; each character is proficient in **two abilities** (one from class, one from background) and adds the proficiency bonus to any check using those abilities. (An "Ability Proficiencies by Class" table suggests class options, e.g., Barbarian: Str/Dex/Wis; Bard: any one.) This removes per-skill distinctions — a character can't favor Persuasion over Intimidation.
- **Background Proficiency** — no skill/tool proficiencies; instead add the proficiency bonus to any check the character's **background** reasonably covers, with the DM as judge. Players should justify the relevance specifically.
- **Personality Trait Proficiency** — add the bonus to checks tied to a **positive personality trait**; a relevant **negative trait** imposes disadvantage. Players define at least four positive traits.

These also rework the **Expertise** feature accordingly (it applies to an ability/background/trait rather than to two skills).

### Hero Points (optional) — DMG p.265

For epic/mythic games where PCs are more superhero than ordinary adventurer.

- A character starts with **5 hero points at 1st level**.
- On each level-up, the character **loses any unspent points** and gains a new total equal to **5 + half the character's level**.
- A player may spend **1 hero point per roll** (max one) on an attack roll, ability check, or saving throw — **after the roll, before results apply** — to **roll a d6 and add it** to the d20.
- A hero point can also turn a **failed death saving throw into a success**.

### Honor score (optional new ability) — DMG p.264–265

A seventh ability for campaigns built on a rigid code of honor (e.g., Asian-inspired settings, knightly orders). Measures devotion to and understanding of the code, and how others perceive the character's honor. Unlike the six standard abilities, **Honor can't be raised by normal ability-score increases** — the DM raises or lowers it by 1 at the end of an adventure based on the character's conduct (cannot exceed 20 or fall below 1). Used for **Honor checks** (social situations governed by a code) and **Honor saving throws** (resisting an accidental breach, goading, or being tricked into dishonor).

### Sanity score (optional new ability) — DMG p.265–266

A seventh/eighth ability for cosmic-horror campaigns (alien entities, Far Realm). High Sanity = level-headed under madness; low Sanity = breaks easily. Used for **Sanity checks** (recalling forbidden lore, deciphering alien text, learning spells from forbidden tomes) and **Sanity saving throws** (seeing a Far Realm creature, mind contact with an alien creature, mind-affecting spells, psychic damage). A failed save can trigger short-term, long-term, or indefinite **madness** (rules in Ch.8, "Running the Game," p.258). Long-term or indefinite madness **reduces Sanity by 1**; *greater restoration* (or level advancement) can restore it.

**Adding an optional ability at creation:** standard array → add an extra **11**; point-buy → add **3 points**; rolled → roll for the new ability. For a monster lacking the score, substitute **Charisma for Honor** and **Wisdom for Sanity**.

*Full detail: DMG p.263–266.*

## Adventuring options (DMG p.266–267)

> **Note on identifying magic items.** The prompt groups this here, but the **"More Difficult Identification"** variant actually lives in Chapter 7 (Treasure), **DMG p.136**. It removes the default ability to learn a magic item's properties during a short rest, requiring the *identify* spell, experimentation, or both instead. (Default 5e also requires **attunement** before some items' properties can be used — DMG p.136–138.)

### Fear and Horror (optional) — DMG p.266

For dark-fantasy dread. **Fear:** a Wisdom save (DC by circumstance); failure = frightened 1 minute, repeat save at end of each turn. **Horror:** a Charisma save; failure = a short- or long-term form of **madness**.

### Healing variants (optional) — DMG p.266–267

- **Healer's Kit Dependency** — a character can't spend Hit Dice after a short rest until someone uses one charge of a **healer's kit** on them.
- **Slow Natural Healing** — characters **don't regain HP at the end of a long rest**; instead they spend Hit Dice to heal during a long rest, exactly as during a short rest. Grittier, slower recovery without magic.
- **Healing Surges** — as an **action**, a character spends up to **half their Hit Dice**, rolling each and adding the Con modifier to regain HP (deciding after each roll whether to spend another). Can't use again until a short or long rest. (For a superheroic feel, allow it as a **bonus action** instead.)
- **Lingering Injuries** — pointer: this option lives in the Combat Options section below (DMG p.272). It introduces lasting wounds from crits, dropping to 0 HP, and big failed death saves.

### Rest variants (optional) — DMG p.267

Default 5e rests (short = 1 hour, long = 8 hours) suit heroic play. Two alternates change the *durations* (verify these exact numbers — they are easy to misremember):

| Variant | Short rest | Long rest | Feel |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Epic Heroism** | **5 minutes** | **1 hour** | Combat becomes routine; consider making encounters harder. |
| **Gritty Realism** | **8 hours** | **7 days** | Slows the campaign; rewards careful planning and downtime. |

Under **Epic Heroism**, the DMG suggests reining in spellcasters: on a long rest restore only **half** maximum spell slots (rounded down), capped at **5th level or lower**; only a full **8-hour rest** restores all slots and any of **6th level or higher** (DMG p.267).

### Other adventuring options (optional) — DMG p.267–269

Brief pointers: **Firearms** (Renaissance/modern/futuristic; properties: ammunition, burst fire, reload — DMG p.267–268), **Explosives** (bomb, dynamite, grenades, gunpowder — DMG p.267–268), **Alien Technology** (Intelligence-check series to figure out — DMG p.268), and **Plot Points** (players spend points to alter the story or even take over as DM — DMG p.269–270).

*Full detail: DMG p.266–270.*

## Combat options (DMG p.270–272)

> Main risk of these: **slowing down play**. Add them singly or as a group.

### Initiative variants (optional) — DMG p.270–271

- **Initiative Score** — no initiative rolls; each creature uses a passive Dexterity check, **10 + Dex modifier**, as a fixed initiative score. Faster, but predictable.
- **Side Initiative** — the players (as one side) roll a single **d20**, and the DM rolls a **d20** for the monsters — **no modifiers**. Highest wins; reroll ties. Each side acts in any order it likes before the other side goes. Speeds combat and aids monster coordination, but lets the winning side gang up before enemies act.
- **Speed Factor** — everyone **re-rolls initiative each round** after first declaring an action, applying modifiers from the Speed Factor table (e.g., **spellcasting: −(spell level)**; melee light/finesse: +2; melee heavy: −2; two-handed: −2; ranged loading: −5; size from Tiny +5 down to Gargantuan −8). The DM counts down from 30; ties broken by higher Dex. Adds uncertainty at the cost of speed.

### Action options (optional) — DMG p.271–272

New actions you can grant in combat:

- **Climb onto a Bigger Creature** — treat a Huge/Gargantuan foe as terrain. After getting into position, use your action for a **Str (Athletics) or Dex (Acrobatics) check contested by the target's Dex (Acrobatics)**. Win = you move into its space and cling on, moving with it (its space is difficult terrain) and gaining **advantage on attacks** against it. The big creature can dislodge you with a contested Strength check.
- **Disarm** — a weapon attack roll **contested by the target's Str (Athletics) or Dex (Acrobatics)**; win = no damage but the target drops the item. Attacker has disadvantage if the item is held in two-plus hands; defender gets advantage if larger, disadvantage if smaller.
- **Mark** — when you make a melee attack you can mark the target; until the end of your next turn, your **opportunity attack against it has advantage** and **doesn't cost your reaction** (still limited to one opportunity attack per turn, and blocked by anything that stops reactions).
- **Overrun** — to move through a hostile creature's space, use an **action or bonus action** to make a **Str (Athletics) contest**; win = you move through its space once this turn. Advantage if larger, disadvantage if smaller.
- **Shove Aside** — use the PHB shove attack to push a target **5 feet to the side** (into a space within reach) instead of straight away, at **disadvantage** on the Str (Athletics) check.
- **Tumble** — use an **action or bonus action** for a **Dex (Acrobatics) contest** to move through a hostile creature's space once this turn.

### Hitting cover (optional) — DMG p.272

When a ranged attack **misses** a target that has cover, check whether the roll would have hit without the cover (i.e., the roll is high enough to beat the target's AC but low enough to be stopped by the cover). If so, the **cover object is struck**; if a creature is providing the cover and the roll beats *its* AC, that covering creature is hit.

### Cleaving through creatures (optional) — DMG p.272

For grinding through hordes: when a **melee attack drops an undamaged creature to 0 HP**, any **excess damage carries over** to another creature within reach (if the original roll could hit it). Repeat the carry-over until there's no valid target or the leftover damage no longer drops an undamaged creature to 0.

### Injury and death options (optional) — DMG p.272–273

- **Lingering Injuries** — roll on the **Lingering Injuries table (d20)** when a creature takes a critical hit, drops to 0 HP without dying, or **fails a death save by 5 or more** (DM's call when to check). Results range from a limp or lost eye/limb to internal injury, festering wound, or a minor scar. (The full table is large — pointer it; DMG p.272.) Optionally, the player instead gains a matching **flaw** to roleplay.
- **Massive Damage** — when a single source deals **damage ≥ half the creature's HP maximum**, it makes a **DC 15 Con save** or rolls on the **System Shock table (d10)** — effects run from dropping to 0 HP (1) down to merely losing reactions (8–10).
- **Morale** — to decide when monsters/NPCs flee, make a **DC 10 Wisdom save** (with disadvantage, or auto-fail, against overwhelming odds) under triggering conditions (surprised; reduced to half HP for the first time; a group's leader downed; a group cut to half its size). Failure = flee, or surrender if escape is impossible.

### Flanking, diagonals, and the grid (optional)

> These three are printed in **Chapter 8 ("Running the Game"), DMG p.251–252**, in the "Using a Grid" material — not in Chapter 9. They are still optional combat rules, included here because the prompt groups them with combat options. **Pages cited are the accurate Ch.8 pages.**

- **Flanking (optional)** — DMG p.251. If you use miniatures, flanking grants **advantage on melee attack rolls** against a shared enemy. **On squares:** a creature and an ally on **opposite sides or corners** of the enemy's space flank it (trace a line between the creatures' centers; if it crosses opposite sides/corners, it's a flank). **On hexes:** allies on opposite sides flank — count hexes around the enemy (Medium-or-smaller needs 2 hexes between; Large 4; Huge 5; Gargantuan 6+). A creature **can't flank while it can't see the enemy or is incapacitated**; a Large+ creature flanks if **any one** square/hex of its space qualifies.
- **Diagonals (optional)** — DMG p.252. Replaces the default "every square is 5 ft." movement with **5–10–5**: the first diagonal square costs **5 feet**, the second **10 feet**, alternating thereafter (even across straight movement). More realistic, more bookkeeping.
- **Facing (optional)** — DMG p.252. Track front/side/rear arcs; a creature normally targets only its front and side arcs, and an attacker in its **rear arc has advantage**.
- **Playing on a grid generally** — the default grid method (count every square as 5 ft, including diagonals) is itself in Ch.8 (DMG p.250–252); the items above are the optional refinements to it.

*Full detail: DMG p.270–272 (and DMG p.251–252 for grid options).*

## Design procedures: creating your own content (DMG p.273–289)

These sections are **how-to guides for homebrewing**, not rules to switch on. They lean on big reference tables that aren't reproduced here.

### Creating a monster — DMG p.273–283

Covers (1) **modifying an existing monster** (swap weapons, add a special trait, reskin a stat block — cosmetic changes like languages/alignment/senses don't change CR, but changing HP or damage can); (2) **quick monster stats** by picking an expected CR and reading off combat values; and (3) building a **full monster stat block** from scratch, including a worked **defensive/offensive CR** calculation. The core reference is the **Monster Statistics by Challenge Rating table (CR 0–30)** — proficiency bonus, AC, HP, attack bonus, damage/round, save DC by CR. **That table is large — pointer it (DMG p.274–275); consult the DMG directly when you need the numbers.** The section also walks through building NPCs from monster stat blocks.

> **Creating an NPC / a contest / a trap** — these are *not* in Chapter 9. The prompt lists them, but: **NPC creation** is Chapter 4 (DMG p.89+), **traps** are Chapter 5 (DMG p.120–122), and **contests** are a core PHB mechanic (PHB p.174). Use those references instead.

### Creating a spell — DMG p.283–284

Guidance on balancing a new spell against existing ones, using the **Spell Damage table** (by spell level, for one target vs. multiple targets — e.g., a damaging cantrip ≈ 1d10 single / 1d6 multi; assumes half damage on a save, +25% if it deals none on a save). The same table sizes **healing** spells (no healing cantrips). Pointer the table itself (DMG p.283–284).

### Creating a magic item — DMG p.284–285

How to **modify an existing item** (reskin the weapon, change damage type, swap one capability for another, or fuse two items) or **create one from scratch**, with notes on power level, charges, and rarity. Keep items simple to run.

### Creating new character options — DMG p.285–289

How to homebrew **races/subraces** (cosmetic vs. cultural alterations, new subraces, and a worked eladrin example), **classes**, and **backgrounds** — with repeated emphasis on comparing new options against existing ones so they neither outshine nor pale beside the PHB defaults.

*Full detail: DMG p.273–289.*

## Common questions

**Q: Are any of these rules "on" by default?**
A: No. Every rule in Chapter 9 is optional or a variant; none is part of standard 5e (2014). The DM must explicitly adopt each one (DMG p.263).

**Q: How many Hero Points does a character have?**
A: 5 at 1st level; on each level-up they lose unspent points and reset to **5 + half their level**. Spend 1 per roll (after rolling, before results) to add a **d6**, or to flip a failed death save (DMG p.265).

**Q: What are the exact rest durations under the rest variants?**
A: **Epic Heroism** — short rest **5 minutes**, long rest **1 hour**. **Gritty Realism** — short rest **8 hours**, long rest **7 days** (DMG p.267).

**Q: How does flanking work, and what does it grant?**
A: With miniatures, two allies on opposite sides/corners of an enemy each get **advantage on melee attack rolls** against it. It's an optional rule printed in Chapter 8, **DMG p.251** (not Chapter 9) (DMG p.251).

**Q: Under Proficiency Dice, what replaces a +2 proficiency bonus?**
A: A **1d4** at levels 1–4; the die scales up to 1d12 at levels 17–20. Doubling proficiency (e.g., Expertise) means rolling the die twice (DMG p.263).

**Q: Where do I find the rules for creating an NPC or a trap?**
A: Not in this chapter. NPC creation is **Chapter 4 (DMG p.89+)** and trap design is **Chapter 5 (DMG p.120–122)**. Chapter 9's design content is monsters, spells, magic items, and character options (DMG p.273–289).

**Q: The Lingering Injuries / Monster-by-CR / Spell Damage tables aren't fully here — where are they?**
A: They're large reference tables left as pointers: Lingering Injuries (DMG p.272), Monster Statistics by Challenge Rating (DMG p.274–275), Spell Damage (DMG p.283–284). Consult the DMG directly when you need the exact rows.
