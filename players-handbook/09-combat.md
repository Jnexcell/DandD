# Combat

The core rules for resolving fights: the order of combat (rounds, initiative, surprise), what you can do on your turn, movement and position, the standard combat actions, making attacks, cover, and damage, healing, and death.

**Source:** PHB Chapter 9, "Combat" (printed pages 189–198).

---

## The order of combat

Combat is organized into a cycle of **rounds** and **turns**. A round represents about **6 seconds** in the game world. During a round, every participant takes a turn. The order of turns is set at the start of the encounter, when everyone rolls initiative. Once everyone has acted, the next round begins if neither side has won (PHB p.189).

### Combat step by step (PHB p.189)

1. **Determine surprise.** The DM decides whether anyone is surprised.
2. **Establish positions.** The DM places all combatants based on marching order or stated positions.
3. **Roll initiative.** Everyone involved rolls initiative.
4. **Take turns.** Each participant acts in initiative order.
5. **Begin the next round.** When everyone has had a turn, the round ends. Repeat step 4 until the fight ends.

### Surprise (PHB p.189)

The DM determines who might be surprised. If neither side is being stealthy, both automatically notice each other. Otherwise, the DM compares the **Dexterity (Stealth)** checks of anyone hiding against the **passive Wisdom (Perception)** score of each creature on the opposing side. Any creature that doesn't notice a threat is surprised at the start of the encounter.

If you're surprised, you **can't move or take an action on your first turn** of the combat, and you **can't take a reaction** until that turn ends. A single member of a group can be surprised even if the others aren't.

### Initiative (PHB p.189)

When combat starts, each participant makes a **Dexterity check** to set their place in the initiative order. The DM makes one roll for an entire group of identical creatures. Combatants are ranked from highest total to lowest, and that order stays the same from round to round.

**Ties:** the DM decides the order among tied DM-controlled creatures; players decide among tied characters; the DM breaks ties between a monster and a player character. Optionally, tied combatants each roll a d20, highest going first.

## Your turn

On your turn you can **move a distance up to your speed** and **take one action**. You decide whether to move or act first (PHB p.189). You may also be able to take a bonus action and one free object interaction, and you can split your movement around your action.

You can forgo moving, acting, or doing anything at all. If you can't decide, consider the **Dodge** or **Ready** action.

### Bonus actions (PHB p.189)

Some class features, spells, and abilities let you take an extra action called a **bonus action**. You can take a bonus action only when a feature explicitly allows it, and only **one** per turn. You choose when to take it during your turn unless its timing is specified. Anything that prevents you from taking actions also prevents bonus actions.

### Other activity on your turn (PHB p.190)

Your turn can include flourishes that need neither your action nor your move: you can **communicate** through brief words and gestures, and you can **interact with one object or feature of the environment for free** (during your move or action) — for example, drawing a weapon as part of the attack you make, or opening a door as you move past it. Interacting with a **second** object requires the Use an Object action. The DM may require an action for an interaction that needs special care (e.g., prying open a stuck door).

### Reactions (PHB p.190)

A **reaction** is an instant response to a trigger, which can occur on your turn or someone else's. The most common reaction is the **opportunity attack**. Once you take a reaction, you can't take another until the start of your next turn. If a reaction interrupts another creature's turn, that creature resumes its turn right after the reaction resolves.

## Movement and position

You can use as much or as little of your speed as you like. Movement can include walking, jumping, climbing, and swimming; you deduct the distance of each part from your speed until it's used up (PHB p.190).

- **Breaking up your move.** You can move some of your speed before your action and some after. With a speed of 30 ft., you could move 10 ft., act, then move 20 ft. If your action includes more than one attack, you can also move **between** those attacks (PHB p.190).
- **Using different speeds.** If you have more than one speed (e.g., walking and flying), you can switch between them during a move. When you switch, subtract the distance already moved from the new speed; if the result is 0 or less, you can't use the new speed this move (PHB p.191).
- **Difficult terrain.** Every foot of movement in difficult terrain costs **1 extra foot**, even if multiple things in a space count as difficult terrain. Examples: low furniture, rubble, undergrowth, steep stairs, snow, shallow bogs, and **another creature's space** (PHB p.191).
- **Being prone.** You can **drop prone for free**. Standing up costs **half your speed** (e.g., 15 ft. for a 30-ft. speed); you can't stand if you lack the movement or your speed is 0. While prone you can only **crawl** (or use magic like teleportation), and crawling costs **1 extra foot per foot** (so 3 ft. per foot in difficult terrain) (PHB p.191).
- **Moving around other creatures.** You can move through a **nonhostile** creature's space. You can move through a **hostile** creature's space only if it is at least **two sizes** larger or smaller than you. A creature's space is difficult terrain. You can't willingly **end** your move in another creature's space. Leaving a hostile creature's reach provokes an opportunity attack (PHB p.191).
- **Flying movement.** A flying creature that is knocked prone, reduced to 0 speed, or otherwise unable to move **falls**, unless it can hover or is held aloft by magic (e.g., the *fly* spell) (PHB p.191).

### Creature size and space (PHB p.191–192)

A creature's space is the area it controls in combat, not its literal width.

| Size | Space |
|---|---|
| Tiny | 2½ × 2½ ft. |
| Small | 5 × 5 ft. |
| Medium | 5 × 5 ft. |
| Large | 10 × 10 ft. |
| Huge | 15 × 15 ft. |
| Gargantuan | 20 × 20 ft. or larger |

**Squeezing into a smaller space:** a creature can squeeze through a space large enough for a creature **one size smaller** (e.g., a Large creature through a 5-ft.-wide passage). While squeezing, it spends **1 extra foot per foot moved**, has **disadvantage on attack rolls and Dexterity saving throws**, and **attack rolls against it have advantage** (PHB p.192).

## Actions in combat

When you take your action, you can use one of the standard actions below, an action from your class or a feature, or an improvised action (PHB p.192–193).

| Action | What it does |
|---|---|
| **Attack** | Make one melee or ranged attack. Some features (e.g., Extra Attack) allow more than one attack with this action. |
| **Cast a Spell** | Cast a spell with a casting time of 1 action (see Chapter 10). Not all spells use an action. |
| **Dash** | Gain extra movement for the turn equal to your speed (after modifiers). A 30-ft. speed lets you move up to 60 ft. when you Dash. |
| **Disengage** | Your movement doesn't provoke opportunity attacks for the rest of the turn. |
| **Dodge** | Until the start of your next turn, attacks against you have disadvantage (if you can see the attacker) and you make Dexterity saves with advantage. Lost if you're incapacitated or your speed is 0. |
| **Help** | Give an ally **advantage** on their next ability check for a task you aid, or — if the target is within 5 ft. — grant advantage on the first attack roll an ally makes against it before your next turn. |
| **Hide** | Make a **Dexterity (Stealth)** check to hide. On a success, you gain the benefits of being unseen (see Unseen Attackers and Targets). |
| **Ready** | Choose a trigger and a response (an action, or moving up to your speed). When the trigger occurs, use your reaction to act. Readying a spell requires concentration on the held spell. |
| **Search** | Devote your attention to finding something; the DM may call for a **Wisdom (Perception)** or **Intelligence (Investigation)** check. |
| **Use an Object** | Interact with an object that requires an action, or interact with a **second** object on your turn. |

> Some grapple and shove maneuvers are made as part of the **Attack** action — see [Melee special attacks](#melee-special-attacks-grappling-and-shoving) below.

## Making an attack

Every attack follows a simple structure (PHB p.193–194):

1. **Choose a target** within range (a creature, object, or location).
2. **Determine modifiers** — cover, advantage/disadvantage, and any bonuses or penalties.
3. **Resolve the attack** — make the attack roll; on a hit, roll damage.

> If you're ever unsure whether something counts as an attack, the rule is simple: **if you're making an attack roll, you're making an attack.**

### Attack rolls (PHB p.194)

Roll a **d20** and add the appropriate modifiers. If the total **equals or exceeds** the target's Armor Class (AC), the attack hits.

**Modifiers to the roll:**
- **Ability modifier.** Melee weapon attacks use **Strength**; ranged weapon attacks use **Dexterity**. Weapons with the **finesse** or **thrown** property break this rule. Spell attacks use the caster's spellcasting ability.
- **Proficiency bonus.** Add it when attacking with a weapon you're proficient with, or when attacking with a spell.

### Rolling 1 or 20 (PHB p.194)

- A natural **20** on the attack die **always hits**, regardless of modifiers or AC, and is a **critical hit**.
- A natural **1** on the attack die **always misses**, regardless of modifiers or AC.

### Unseen attackers and targets (PHB p.194–195)

- Attacking a target you **can't see** gives you **disadvantage** on the attack roll (whether you're guessing its location or can hear but not see it). If the target isn't where you aimed, you automatically miss.
- When a creature **can't see you**, you have **advantage** on attacks against it.
- If you are **hidden** (unseen and unheard) when you attack, you **give away your location** whether the attack hits or misses.

### Ranged attacks (PHB p.194–195)

- You can make ranged attacks only against targets **within range**. A single-range attack can't reach beyond that range.
- Weapons with **two ranges** (e.g., a longbow) list a **normal range** and a **long range**. You have **disadvantage** beyond normal range, and you **can't attack** beyond long range.
- **Ranged attacks in close combat:** you have **disadvantage** on a ranged attack if you are within **5 feet** of a hostile creature who can see you and isn't incapacitated.

### Melee attacks and reach (PHB p.195)

A melee attack lets you strike a foe within your reach. Most creatures have a **5-foot reach**. Some creatures (typically larger than Medium) have greater reach, as noted in their descriptions. When unarmed, you can make an **unarmed strike** (see the weapon table in Chapter 5).

### Opportunity attacks (PHB p.195)

You can make an opportunity attack when a hostile creature **you can see moves out of your reach**. Use your **reaction** to make **one melee attack** against it; the attack interrupts the creature's movement, occurring right before it leaves your reach.

You **avoid** provoking an opportunity attack by taking the **Disengage** action. You also don't provoke one when you **teleport** or when something **moves you** without using your own movement, action, or reaction (e.g., being hurled by an explosion or falling past an enemy).

## Melee special attacks (Grappling and Shoving)

Both are special melee attacks made using the **Attack action**; if you can make multiple attacks with the Attack action, one of these replaces one of those attacks (PHB p.195).

### Grappling (PHB p.195)

The target must be **no more than one size larger** than you and **within your reach**. Using at least one free hand, make a **grapple check: Strength (Athletics) contested by the target's Strength (Athletics) or Dexterity (Acrobatics)** (target chooses). On a success, the target is **grappled**. You can release it at any time (no action required).

- **Escaping a grapple:** the grappled creature uses its **action** to make a **Strength (Athletics) or Dexterity (Acrobatics)** check contested by your **Strength (Athletics)** check.
- **Moving a grappled creature:** when you move, you can drag or carry it, but your speed is **halved** — unless the creature is two or more sizes smaller than you.

### Shoving a creature (PHB p.195)

The target must be **no more than one size larger** than you and **within your reach**. Make a **Strength (Athletics)** check contested by the target's **Strength (Athletics) or Dexterity (Acrobatics)** (target chooses). If you win, you either **knock the target prone** or **push it 5 feet** away from you.

## Cover (PHB p.196)

A target benefits from cover only when the attack or effect originates on the **opposite side** of the cover. If multiple sources of cover apply, only the **most protective** degree counts (degrees are not added together).

| Degree | Benefit | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Half cover** | +2 to AC and Dexterity saving throws | Low wall, large furniture, narrow tree trunk, another creature |
| **Three-quarters cover** | +5 to AC and Dexterity saving throws | Portcullis, arrow slit, thick tree trunk |
| **Total cover** | Can't be targeted directly by an attack or spell (but an area of effect can still reach it) | Target completely concealed by an obstacle |

## Damage and healing

### Hit points (PHB p.196)

Hit points represent durability, will to live, and luck. A creature's current hit points can be anything from its hit point maximum down to 0. Losing hit points has **no effect on capabilities** until the creature drops to **0**.

### Damage rolls (PHB p.196)

Roll the weapon's, spell's, or ability's damage dice, add modifiers, and apply the damage. When attacking with a **weapon**, add the **same ability modifier** used for the attack roll. If an effect damages **more than one target at once**, roll the damage **once** for all of them.

### Critical hits (PHB p.196)

On a critical hit, **roll all of the attack's damage dice twice** and add them together, then add relevant modifiers as normal. Example: a critical hit with a dagger rolls **2d4** instead of 1d4, then adds your modifier. Extra dice (e.g., Sneak Attack) are also rolled twice.

### Damage types (PHB p.196–197)

Damage types have no rules of their own, but features like resistance and vulnerability rely on them:

> Acid, Bludgeoning, Cold, Fire, Force, Lightning, Necrotic, Piercing, Poison, Psychic, Radiant, Slashing, Thunder.

### Resistance and vulnerability (PHB p.197)

- **Resistance** to a damage type: damage of that type is **halved**.
- **Vulnerability** to a damage type: damage of that type is **doubled**.

Resistance and then vulnerability are applied **after all other modifiers** to damage. Multiple instances of resistance (or vulnerability) to the same type count as only **one** — damage is never reduced by more than half from stacked resistances. Example: a creature with resistance to bludgeoning takes 25 bludgeoning damage while in an aura that reduces all damage by 5 → first reduce by 5 (to 20), then halve → **10 damage**.

### Healing (PHB p.197)

Healing adds hit points to current hit points, but a creature can't exceed its **hit point maximum** (excess is lost). A creature that has **died** can't regain hit points until magic such as *revivify* restores it to life.

### Dropping to 0 hit points (PHB p.197)

When you drop to 0 hit points, you either die outright or fall unconscious.

- **Instant death.** If damage reduces you to 0 and there is damage remaining, you **die** if the remaining damage **equals or exceeds your hit point maximum**. Example: a cleric with a 12 HP maximum and 6 current HP takes 18 damage — 12 damage remains after reaching 0, which equals her maximum, so she dies.
- **Falling unconscious.** If damage reduces you to 0 but doesn't kill you, you fall **unconscious** (see [conditions](../quick-reference/conditions.md)). This ends when you regain any hit points.

### Death saving throws (PHB p.197)

Whenever you **start your turn with 0 hit points**, make a **death saving throw** (not tied to any ability score):

- Roll a **d20**. **10 or higher** = success; otherwise a failure.
- On your **3rd success**, you become **stable**. On your **3rd failure**, you **die**. Successes and failures need not be consecutive; track both until you reach three of a kind.
- The count **resets to zero** when you regain any hit points or become stable.
- **Natural 1** counts as **two failures**. **Natural 20** means you **regain 1 hit point** (and become conscious).
- **Damage at 0 HP:** taking any damage while at 0 HP causes **one** death save failure; a **critical hit** causes **two**. Damage that equals or exceeds your hit point maximum causes **instant death**.

### Stabilizing a creature (PHB p.197)

Use your **action** to administer first aid with a successful **DC 10 Wisdom (Medicine)** check. A stable creature doesn't make death saving throws but remains **unconscious**. It stops being stable (and resumes death saves) if it takes any damage. A stable creature that isn't healed **regains 1 hit point after 1d4 hours**.

### Monsters and death (PHB p.198)

Most DMs have a monster **die instantly** when it drops to 0 hit points, rather than making death saving throws. Mighty villains and special NPCs are common exceptions and may follow the player-character rules.

### Knocking a creature out (PHB p.198)

When you reduce a creature to 0 hit points with a **melee attack**, you can choose to **knock it out** instead of killing it (decided the instant damage is dealt). The creature falls **unconscious and is stable**.

### Temporary hit points (PHB p.198)

Temporary hit points are a **buffer** against damage, not actual hit points.

- When you take damage, **temporary hit points are lost first**; leftover damage carries over to your normal hit points.
- They **can exceed your hit point maximum** (you can be at full HP and still gain them).
- Healing **can't restore** temporary hit points, and they **don't stack** — if you gain more while you already have some, you choose which set to keep (not the sum).
- Gaining them at **0 HP** doesn't restore consciousness or stabilize you, but they can still absorb incoming damage.
- Unless the source gives a duration, they last **until depleted or until you finish a long rest**.

## Mounted combat (PHB p.198)

A **willing** creature at least **one size larger** than you with appropriate anatomy can serve as a mount.

- **Mounting/dismounting** costs movement equal to **half your speed** and can be done once during your move.
- If an effect moves your mount against its will while you're on it, or you're knocked prone while mounted, make a **DC 10 Dexterity saving throw** or fall off, landing prone within 5 ft. of it.
- If your mount is knocked prone, you can use your **reaction** to dismount as it falls and land on your feet.
- **Controlled mount:** its initiative changes to match yours, it moves as you direct, and it has only three action options — **Dash, Disengage, Dodge**. You can control a mount only if it's trained to accept a rider.
- **Independent mount** (e.g., an intelligent creature): keeps its own initiative and acts as it wishes.
- If a mounted creature provokes an opportunity attack, the attacker can target **you or the mount**.

## Underwater combat (PHB p.198)

- A **melee weapon attack** by a creature without a swimming speed has **disadvantage**, unless the weapon is a **dagger, javelin, shortsword, spear, or trident**.
- A **ranged weapon attack** automatically **misses** beyond the weapon's normal range; within normal range it has **disadvantage** unless the weapon is a **crossbow, a net, or a thrown weapon like a javelin** (including a spear, trident, or dart).
- Creatures and objects **fully immersed in water** have **resistance to fire damage**.

## Common questions

**Q: If I'm surprised, can I do anything on the first round?**
A: No movement and no action on your first turn, and you can't take reactions (including opportunity attacks) until that turn ends (PHB p.189). Surprise affects the first turn, not the whole combat.

**Q: How much movement does standing up from prone cost?**
A: Half your speed (e.g., 15 ft. for a 30-ft. speed). Dropping prone is free (PHB p.191).

**Q: Does a critical hit double my whole damage, including my ability modifier?**
A: No — you double only the **dice**, then add modifiers once. A crit with a longsword is 2d8 + your Strength modifier, not (1d8 + Str) × 2 (PHB p.196).

**Q: Who chooses the ability for the contested check when I grapple or shove?**
A: The **target** chooses whether to oppose your Strength (Athletics) with their Strength (Athletics) or Dexterity (Acrobatics) (PHB p.195).

**Q: Does stacking two sources of resistance to the same damage type reduce damage to a quarter?**
A: No. Multiple instances of resistance to the same type count as one, so damage is reduced by half at most (PHB p.197).

**Q: I'm at 0 HP and take damage. How many death save failures is that?**
A: One failure for ordinary damage, two for a critical hit. If the damage equals or exceeds your hit point maximum, you die instantly (PHB p.197).

**Q: Can I make a ranged attack while standing next to an enemy?**
A: Yes, but you have disadvantage if you're within 5 ft. of a hostile creature who can see you and isn't incapacitated (PHB p.195).
