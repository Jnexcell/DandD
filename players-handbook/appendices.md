# Appendices (A–D)

An orientation guide to the four PHB appendices: the rules glossary of **conditions** (A), the **gods of the multiverse** pantheon lists (B), an overview of the **planes of existence** (C), and **stat blocks for common creatures** a character might summon, befriend, or transform into (D).

**Source:** PHB Appendices A–D (printed pages 290–311). The PHB index lists them as: Conditions 290–292, deities 293–299, planes of existence 300–303, creature statistics 304–311. (As noted in the README, the PHB scan's printed→physical offset drifts and the appendix pages were OCR'd without folio numbers, so these citations come from the printed book's own index.)

---

## Appendix A — Conditions (PHB p.290–292)

A **condition** alters a creature's capabilities. It can come from a spell, a class feature, a monster's attack, or another effect. Most (like *blinded*) are impairments; a few (like *invisible*) are advantageous. A condition lasts until it is countered (you stand up to end *prone*) or until a stated duration ends. If the same condition is imposed more than once, each instance has its own duration but the effects don't stack — a creature either has a condition or it doesn't (PHB p.290).

The 14 named conditions are:

**Blinded · Charmed · Deafened · Frightened · Grappled · Incapacitated · Invisible · Paralyzed · Petrified · Poisoned · Prone · Restrained · Stunned · Unconscious**

Separately, **Exhaustion** is a special condition measured in **6 cumulative levels** (PHB p.291). It comes from hazards like starvation, lack of water, forced marching, and extreme cold or heat. Each level adds a worsening penalty (disadvantage on ability checks, halved speed, disadvantage on attacks and saves, lowered hit point maximum, speed reduced to 0, and finally death at level 6). A long rest removes one level, provided the creature has also eaten and drunk enough.

> **Full reference:** the complete bullet-point effects of all 14 conditions and the level-by-level exhaustion table are written out in **[`../quick-reference/conditions.md`](../quick-reference/conditions.md)**. This file is only a pointer — use the cheat sheet for adjudication.

## Appendix B — Gods of the Multiverse (PHB p.293–299)

This appendix is a **set of pantheon lists** so players and DMs can choose a deity for a character to worship — especially relevant for clerics, paladins, and characters with the Acolyte background (PHB p.293). The DM decides which gods exist in their campaign; a player can pick one deity, a few, or a whole pantheon.

Each deity entry gives the same four pieces of information:

- the deity's **name**,
- their **alignment**,
- their **suggested cleric domain(s)** (Knowledge, Life, Light, Nature, Tempest, Trickery, War — the seven PHB domains; the Death domain lives in the DMG),
- and their **symbol**.

The appendix covers four published **campaign-setting pantheons** plus four **historical (real-world) pantheons**:

| Pantheon | Notes |
|---|---|
| **The Forgotten Realms** | The largest list — at least 30 widely known deities (e.g., Tymora, goddess of good fortune; Tempus, god of war). |
| **Greyhawk** | Gods drawn from several ethnic pantheons, with overlapping portfolios (e.g., two separate sun gods, Pelor and Pholtus). |
| **Dragonlance** | The gods of Krynn — three families of seven (good under Paladine/Mishakal, neutral under Gilean, evil under Takhisis/Sargonnas). |
| **Eberron** | Centered on the **Sovereign Host** and its dark counterpart the **Dark Six**, plus other faiths (the Silver Flame, the Blood of Vol, the Path of Light). |
| **Celtic** | Historical pantheon (e.g., The Daghdha, Silvanus). |
| **Egyptian** | Historical; unusual for having three families of gods (good, evil, and the dead). |
| **Greek (Olympian)** | Historical (e.g., Zeus, Athena, Apollo). |
| **Norse** | Historical; two families, the Aesir and the Vanir. |

The appendix also notes **nonhuman deities** shared across worlds — e.g., **Moradin** is revered by dwarves in the Forgotten Realms, Greyhawk, and beyond — and explains that nonhuman races often have whole pantheons of their own (PHB p.296–299).

> The full deity tables are not reproduced here (length and copyright). Look up the specific god on PHB p.293–299.

## Appendix C — The Planes of Existence (PHB p.300–303)

A **player-facing overview** of the D&D cosmos: the alternate dimensions of reality, called the planes of existence, that spells and magic items reach into to summon creatures, draw energy, or send adventurers traveling (PHB p.300).

- **The Material Plane** is the nexus and starting point for most campaigns; every D&D world (Greyhawk, Forgotten Realms, Eberron, Dragonlance, and the rest) exists within it. The whole rest of the multiverse is defined in relation to it.
- **The echo planes** parallel the Material Plane and share its space:
  - the **Feywild** (Plane of Faerie) — a brighter, more marvelous and magical mirror, a realm of eternal twilight ruled by the Seelie and Unseelie Courts.
  - the **Shadowfell** (Plane of Shadow) — a distorted, colorless, gloom-leached mirror.
- **The Transitive Planes** are mostly featureless realms used for travel: the **Ethereal Plane** (a misty dimension whose Border Ethereal overlaps the Material and Inner Planes) and the **Astral Plane** (a silvery sea of thought reached by *astral projection*).
- **The Inner Planes** are the raw elemental substance of creation: the four **Elemental Planes** (Air, Earth, Fire, Water) ringing the Material Plane, suspended in the churning **Elemental Chaos**. The **Positive** and **Negative Planes** are the sources of life-giving radiant energy and undeath-fueling necrotic energy.
- **The Outer Planes** are the divine/spiritual realms — the homes of the gods. The 16 best-known correspond to the eight non-neutral alignments and the shades between them (Mount Celestia, Elysium, Arborea, Ysgard, Limbo, the Abyss, the Nine Hells, Mechanus, and more). Arranged in a ring, they form the cosmology known as the **Great Wheel**, with the neutral **Outlands** at the hub and the ring-shaped city of **Sigil**, the City of Doors, floating above its central Spire.

Travel between planes happens by **spell** (*plane shift*, *gate*, *etherealness*, *astral projection*) or through a **portal** (a doorway, vortex, or location existing in multiple planes at once) (PHB p.300–301).

> **Full treatment:** this appendix is the short, player-facing version. The DMG goes into the planes in depth — their traits, how travel works, and the layers of each plane. See **[`../dungeon-masters-guide/01-02-world-building.md`](../dungeon-masters-guide/01-02-world-building.md)** (DMG Chapter 2, "Creating a Multiverse").

## Appendix D — Creature Statistics (PHB p.304–311)

Stat blocks for the **creatures the PHB itself references** — gathered here for convenience so players don't need the Monster Manual at the table. They cover three uses (PHB p.304):

- **Familiars** (*find familiar* lets a wizard summon a tiny beast — bat, cat, owl, rat, etc.).
- **Mounts and companions** (*find steed*, the Beast Master ranger, the *animal friendship*/*conjure animals* spells, and similar features).
- **Conjured, transformed, or created creatures** (e.g., *conjure animals*, druid *Wild Shape* options, and undead raised by *animate dead*).

The creatures with stat blocks in this appendix are:

**Bat · Black Bear · Brown Bear · Boar · Cat · Crocodile · Dire Wolf · Frog · Hawk · Imp · Lion · Mastiff · Mule · Owl · Panther · Quasit · Rat · Raven · Reef Shark · Skeleton · Sprite · Tiger · Warhorse · Wolf · Zombie**

(The Imp and Quasit appear here because they are *find familiar* options for warlocks and others.) For how to read a stat block, this appendix points to the Monster Manual.

> The full stat blocks are not reproduced here (length and copyright). Look them up on PHB p.304–311, or use the Monster Manual indexes — see **[`../monster-manual/monster-index.md`](../monster-manual/monster-index.md)** and the stat-block primer in **[`../monster-manual/README.md`](../monster-manual/README.md)**.

## Common questions

**Q: Where do I find the full effects of a condition like *stunned* or *restrained*?**
A: The cheat sheet **[`../quick-reference/conditions.md`](../quick-reference/conditions.md)** has every condition's bullet effects and the full exhaustion table. Appendix A (PHB p.290) is the original source.

**Q: How many levels of exhaustion are there, and what removes them?**
A: Six. Level 6 is death. A long rest removes one level, but only if the creature has also had enough food and drink (PHB p.291).

**Q: My cleric needs a deity — where's the list?**
A: Appendix B, PHB p.293–299. Pick from the pantheon your DM is using (Forgotten Realms, Greyhawk, Dragonlance, Eberron, or a historical pantheon). Each entry lists alignment, suggested domain(s), and symbol.

**Q: What plane is the Feywild / Shadowfell, and where's the deep cosmology?**
A: Both are "echo" planes parallel to the Material Plane (Appendix C, PHB p.300). For the full planar rules and the Great Wheel in detail, see the DMG — **[`../dungeon-masters-guide/01-02-world-building.md`](../dungeon-masters-guide/01-02-world-building.md)**.

**Q: Where are the stats for my familiar / animal companion / Wild Shape form?**
A: Appendix D, PHB p.304–311, has the common beasts (bat, cat, owl, wolf, etc.) plus the imp and quasit familiars. For anything not listed there, use the Monster Manual — **[`../monster-manual/monster-index.md`](../monster-manual/monster-index.md)**.
