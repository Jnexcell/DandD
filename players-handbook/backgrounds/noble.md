# Noble

A character of title and blood — born to wealth, power, and privilege, connected to a family that owns land, collects taxes, and wields real political influence.

**Source:** PHB Chapter 4, "Backgrounds" (printed pages 135–136).

---

## Flavor & lore

You understand wealth, power, and privilege. You carry a noble title, and your family owns land, collects taxes, and wields significant political influence. You might be a pampered aristocrat unfamiliar with work or discomfort, a former merchant freshly elevated to the nobility, a disinherited scoundrel with a disproportionate sense of entitlement, or an honest, hard-working landowner who genuinely cares about the people on your land.

A noble title never stands alone — it's tied to an entire family, and to a web of obligations. **Work with your DM** to pin down your title and how much authority it carries, then sketch the family: Is it old and established or newly raised? How much influence, and over what area? What's your place in the line of inheritance, and how does the head of the family feel about your adventuring? A coat of arms, a signet, family colors, or a heraldic animal all help make your house feel real at the table.

---

## Mechanics

- **Skill Proficiencies:** History, Persuasion
- **Tool Proficiencies:** One type of gaming set
- **Languages:** One of your choice
- **Equipment:** A set of fine clothes, a signet ring, a scroll of pedigree, and a purse containing **25 gp**.

---

## Feature: Position of Privilege

Thanks to your noble birth, people are inclined to think the best of you. You are **welcome in high society**, and people assume you have the right to be wherever you are. The common folk make every effort to accommodate you and avoid your displeasure, and other people of high birth treat you as a member of the same social sphere. You can **secure an audience with a local noble** if you need to. (PHB p.135)

## Variants / specialties

### Variant Noble: Knight (p.136)

A knighthood is among the lowest noble titles in most societies, but it can be a path to higher status. If you want to play a knight, take the **Retainers** feature (below) **instead of** Position of Privilege. One of your three retainers is replaced by a **noble squire** who serves and trains under you; the other two might be a **groom** (to care for your horse) and a **servant** who polishes your armor and helps you don it. As an emblem of chivalry and courtly love, you might carry a **banner or token** from a lord or lady to whom you've given your heart in chaste devotion — this person could be your bond.

### Variant Feature: Retainers

*(Selectable instead of Position of Privilege — used by the Knight variant, but available to any Noble.)*

You have the service of **three retainers** loyal to your family — attendants or messengers, one of whom might be a majordomo. They are **commoners** who can perform mundane tasks for you, but they **do not fight for you**, will not follow you into obviously dangerous areas (such as dungeons), and will leave if frequently endangered or abused. (PHB p.136)

## Suggested characteristics

Nobles are born and raised to a very different lifestyle than most people, and their personalities reflect that upbringing. A title comes with a web of **bonds** — responsibilities to family, to other nobles (including the sovereign), to the people in the family's care, or to the title itself — and that responsibility is often the lever used to undermine a noble.

## Common questions

**Q: How powerful is my title in play?**
A: As powerful as you and the DM agree. The background gives you social access (Position of Privilege), not land armies. Pin down the title's authority and reach with the DM up front so it's a consistent worldbuilding fact, not an on-the-fly ruling. (PHB p.135)

**Q: Do my retainers fight for me?**
A: No. Retainers are commoners for mundane service — they won't fight, won't enter dungeons, and will leave if you keep endangering or abusing them. They're flavor and convenience, not extra combatants. (PHB p.136)

**Q: Knight vs. plain Noble — what actually changes?**
A: Only the feature: a Knight swaps **Position of Privilege** for **Retainers** (one retainer becomes a squire), and the flavor leans chivalric (banner, courtly-love token). Skills, tools, gold, and characteristics are unchanged. (PHB p.136)
