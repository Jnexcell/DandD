# DM Toolkit — Scenes, Goals & Avenues

**Purpose:** keep the *story* moving — build a scene on the fly, give the party real choices ("what are all the ways they could tackle this?"), feed them leads, and cut cleanly to the next beat. This is the **runtime** companion to the MMOS prep framework: MMOS lays out the adventure; this file runs each moment of it.

**Grounded in / pulls from:**
- `quick-reference/one-shot-formula-mmos.md` — the macro structure: **Quest → Two Paths → The Key → Encounters → Showdown → Endings → Hook.** Build the one-shot there; run its beats here.
- `dungeon-masters-guide/03-creating-adventures.md` — adventure structure (location-based vs. event-based), encounters, hooks, and pacing (DMG p.71–88).
- `npc-generator.md` · `encounters-and-monsters.md` · `traps-puzzles-hazards.md` · `social-and-dialog.md` · `environments-and-dressing.md` — the content each scene calls on.

## Scene anatomy (build one in 4 questions)

Every scene worth playing answers these. Decide them in a breath:

1. **Goal** — what do the PCs want *here*? (get past the gate, learn the cult's plan, recover the relic, survive the bridge).
2. **Obstacle** — what's in the way? (a guard, a locked vault, a hostile crowd, a chasm, a riddle).
3. **Stakes** — what's gained or lost? **And a clock** if it's tense: what gets worse if they dawdle? (reinforcements, the ritual completes, the tide comes in).
4. **Exit** — how does the scene *end and hand off*? (a clue points onward, a door opens, the fight breaks, a deal is struck). Know the exit so you can **cut** to it.

> If a scene has a goal, an obstacle, and stakes, it's a scene. If it has none of those, it's a hallway — narrate past it.

## Avenues — always offer more than one way

The heart of player agency (and of MMOS's **Two Paths**): an obstacle should have **at least two viable approaches**, and you reward whichever the players invent. When they propose something, find the avenue and call for the matching check/action.

| Avenue | Looks like | Hand off to |
|---|---|---|
| **Fight** | Force it; kill/subdue the obstacle | `encounters-and-monsters.md` |
| **Stealth** | Sneak past, infiltrate, steal | Stealth vs. passive Perception |
| **Social** | Talk, bluff, bribe, intimidate, persuade | `social-and-dialog.md` |
| **Trickery** | Disguise, forgery, a clever ruse, a distraction | Deception/Sleight of Hand + the fiction |
| **Magic / items** | A spell or tool that sidesteps the problem | `players-handbook/spells/`, the party's gear |
| **Knowledge** | Recall a weakness, decode the lore, find the secret door | Int (Arcana/History/Investigation) |
| **Skill / physical** | Climb, swim, jump, balance, endure → often a **skill challenge** | `traps-puzzles-hazards.md` (skill challenges) |
| **Avoid** | Go around, find another route, leave it for later | hand them a map choice |

**Say yes to the unexpected.** If a player's plan is clever and plausible, let it work (maybe at a cost). The two paths you prepped are a floor, not a ceiling — "**yes, and…**" or "**no, but…**," rarely a flat "no."

## Leads & clues — never leave them stuck

A one-shot dies when the party doesn't know what to do next. Prevent it:

- **Three-clue rule:** for every conclusion the party *must* reach, plant **three** different clues pointing at it. They will miss some.
- **Fail forward:** a failed check costs *time, resources, or a complication* — it never removes the only path. (Failed to pick the lock? The lock holds, **and** the patrol is coming.)
- **The bored-player failsafe:** if they're stalled, have **something happen** — a new arrival, a noise, the villain's agent acts, the clock ticks audibly. Pressure restarts momentum.
- **Bread-crumb every scene's exit** with a hook to the next (a name, a direction, a stolen letter). The MMOS **Key** (Step 3) is the big example: a discoverable thing that unlocks the better ending — seed clues to it across scenes.

## Complications & twists (drop one when a scene goes flat)

Roll/pick a **d8** when you need to escalate or surprise:

| d8 | Complication |
|---|---|
| 1 | Reinforcements / a patrol arrives — the clock just sped up |
| 2 | The environment turns (fire spreads, floor gives way, tide rises) → `environments-and-dressing.md` |
| 3 | An NPC isn't who they claimed (ally is a spy; victim is the villain) |
| 4 | A bystander/hostage complicates the "obvious" violent solution |
| 5 | The thing they came for is gone / already taken / a fake |
| 6 | A moral choice with no clean answer (save these, or chase that) |
| 7 | A rival party/faction wants the same prize |
| 8 | Success has a price — they win, but it costs or reveals something |

Use twists to **raise stakes or open avenues**, not to negate player wins. A twist that makes their choices matter *more* is good; one that makes their choices *pointless* is a railroad.

## Pacing & transitions

- **Cut to the action.** End a scene the moment its goal resolves; skip the walk to the next room. "Twenty minutes later, you reach the chapel doors — what's your plan?"
- **Spotlight rotation.** Make sure each player gets a beat to shine — a lock for the rogue, a lore check for the wizard, a parley for the bard, a brawl for the fighter. The **MMOS Encounters** mix (2 combat / 1 roleplay / 1 exploration) is built for this.
- **Modular on the fly.** MMOS encounters are **skippable** by design — if you're short on time, drop a side scene and head for the **Showdown** (the one non-modular beat). Track the clock against the **Endings** you prepped.
- **Vary the texture:** tense → quiet → funny → tense. Don't run three fights in a row; put a social or exploration beat between them.
- **End on the Hook.** When the Showdown resolves, land the **Ending** that fits their choices and drop the **Hook** (`one-shot-formula-mmos.md` Steps 6–7) — even a one-shot deserves a button.

## 30-second checklist (build a scene live)

1. **Goal · Obstacle · Stakes (+ clock?)** — say them to yourself.
2. **Two+ avenues** — what are the obvious ways through, and stay open to a third.
3. **A lead out** — what clue/exit points to the next beat (three-clue rule for must-finds).
4. **A complication in your back pocket** — to deploy if it stalls or goes flat.
5. **Know your cut** — end on the interesting beat and transition.
