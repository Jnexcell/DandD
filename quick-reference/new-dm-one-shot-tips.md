# New DM: Running Your First One-Shot (Quick Reference)

Practical, encouraging advice for a first-time DM running a single self-contained session. Rules-grounded guidance is cited; widely-used table practices that aren't in the 2014 books are clearly separated at the bottom.

**Source (rules portions):** DMG Chapter 8, "Running the Game" (printed pages 235–240). Everything under "Table practices (not from the rulebook)" is community good-practice, *not* drawn from the 2014 core books.

---

## You're in charge, not the rules

The rules serve you and your players — not the other way around. Their whole job is to keep the fun moving (DMG p.235). When you don't know a rule, **make a fair, fast ruling and keep playing**; look it up later if it mattered. A confident wrong-but-consistent call beats stopping the game to flip through a book.

> **Rulings over rules.** Dice and rules are *tools* to keep the action moving. At any time you can simply decide a player's action automatically succeeds, or grant advantage to reduce the chance a bad roll spoils a clever plan — and a bad plan or rough circumstances can impose disadvantage or make a task impossible (DMG p.237).

---

## When to call for a roll (DMG p.237)

Only ask for an ability check when **both** of these are false:
1. Is the task so easy and low-stakes that there's no real chance of failure?
2. Is it so impossible that it simply can't work?

If a roll *is* warranted, only call for one when **failure has a meaningful consequence**. A character doesn't roll Dexterity to cross an empty room or Charisma to order a drink. **Say yes** to the trivial stuff; save the dice for moments of genuine tension.

### Setting a DC fast
Lean on the Typical DCs as anchors (see `quick-reference/difficulty-classes.md`): **Easy 10, Medium 15, Hard 20** (PHB p.174). When in doubt, 15 is a reliable default for "a real but fair challenge."

## Pacing a one-shot

A one-shot has to **start, climax, and resolve in one sitting**, so pacing matters more than in a campaign:

- **Open in motion.** Skip the slow tavern intro — drop the party straight into or just before the hook.
- **Cut scenes that aren't earning their keep.** If a search or a negotiation is stalling, narrate past it or ask for a single check to resolve it.
- **Watch the clock against your big beats.** Know roughly how long combat takes at your table and budget for the finale.
- **Combat math:** size fights with the XP-budget method in `quick-reference/encounter-building.md`. For a one-shot, one or two **Hard/Deadly** set-pieces are more memorable than a string of easy fights — there's no "next session" to save resources for.

## Table practices (not from the rulebook)

The following are widely-accepted community good practices for one-shots. They are **not** found in the 2014 core books — adopt the ones that fit your table.

### Before the session
- **Session zero / quick safety chat.** Even for a one-shot, take two minutes up front: tone (gritty vs. heroic vs. comedic), content lines (topics that are off the table), and expectations.
- **Safety tools.** Tools like the **X-card** (anyone can tap or say "X" to skip/dial back content, no explanation needed) and **lines & veils** let players steer away from material they don't want, no questions asked. Agree on one before you start.
- **Pre-generated characters.** For a one-shot, hand out **pre-gens** (typically levels 3–5, where classes feel complete but turns stay manageable). It saves 30–60 minutes of character creation and avoids rules-lookup paralysis at the table.
- **Prep a strong hook.** Give the party a single, clear reason to act in the first five minutes — a threat, a contract, a mystery, a deadline. One-shots have no time to meander.
- **Prep less, but prep flexibly.** Know your NPCs' goals and 2–3 key scenes; improvise the connective tissue. Don't script the players' choices.

### During the session
- **"Say yes, or roll the dice."** When a player wants to try something reasonable, default to letting it work (or letting a single roll decide) rather than blocking it. Keep momentum.
- **Spread the spotlight.** Make sure every player gets a moment to shine — a quiet player's idea, a check only their character can make, a scene tailored to them. Gently redirect a dominant player.
- **Telegraph danger.** Hint at lethal threats before they land, so deaths feel earned, not arbitrary.
- **It's okay to fudge sparingly.** Rolling behind a screen lets you nudge a result to protect the story or a doomed PC — but do it rarely and never let on, or players stop feeling real stakes (this *is* discussed in the DMG, p.235).
- **End on a beat.** Aim for a clear climax and a short denouement so the table leaves satisfied.

## A 60-second pre-game checklist

- [ ] Pre-gen characters printed / shared
- [ ] Tone + content lines agreed; safety tool chosen
- [ ] A clear opening hook ready to deliver
- [ ] 2–3 key scenes and the finale sketched out
- [ ] Encounters sized with the XP budget (`encounter-building.md`)
- [ ] Dice, initiative tracker, and a way to track HP/conditions
- [ ] Permission to relax — rulings over rules, and have fun

## Common questions

**Q: A player tries something the rules don't cover. What do I do?**
A: Decide whether it could plausibly work, pick an ability and a DC (default 15), and ask for a check — or just say yes if it's low-stakes. Make the call and move on; that's literally the DM's job (DMG p.235, 237).

**Q: When should I make players roll vs. just letting them succeed?**
A: Only roll when failure is both possible and meaningful. No roll for trivial actions; no roll for the impossible (DMG p.237).

**Q: What level should one-shot pre-gens be?**
A: Community practice favors **levels 3–5** — classes have their core toys but turns stay quick. (This is table advice, not a book rule.)

**Q: Is it cheating to fudge a die roll?**
A: Used sparingly and hidden, the DMG explicitly allows nudging results behind the screen to serve the story — but overusing it erodes the players' sense of real stakes (DMG p.235).

**Q: How do I keep a one-shot from running long?**
A: Open in motion, cut scenes that stall, resolve drawn-out tasks with a single check, and budget time for your finale (DMG p.235–240 on pacing; rest is table practice).
