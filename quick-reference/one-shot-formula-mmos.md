# One-Shot Formula — Pointy Hat's Modular Method (MMOS)

A step-by-step framework for designing a one-shot you can actually **finish in a single session**,
built around **modularity**: most encounters are written so you can *drop them on the fly* to stay on
time. Work through the steps and fill in the blanks (or ask Claude to brainstorm each one with you).

**Source:** "Modular Method for One-Shots (MMOS)" by **Pointy Hat** (third-party design method, not a
WotC rulebook). Pair it with the rules KB when you build the actual content:
- Pacing, rulings-not-rules, safety tools → `new-dm-one-shot-tips.md`
- Stat the fights to the party's level → `encounter-building.md` (XP thresholds & multipliers)
- Monsters → `../monster-manual/` · Difficulty Classes for skill challenges → `difficulty-classes.md`

> **Core idea:** Build a short critical path plus optional side content. Mark **every encounter except
> the finale as modular (skippable)**, so you can cut to length without breaking the story. The running
> example below (a ghost haunting a tavern) threads through all the steps.

---

## Step 0 — Above the Table *(before you play)*

This happens before the session starts, to buy you more time to actually play. Do two things:

1. **Tell your players the Quest up front.** Knowing the goal sets the expectation that the session is
   about *completing that task*, which keeps players from wandering outside the one-shot's scope.
2. **Have players describe their characters to each other** ahead of time, skipping long in-game intros.

Also **communicate the expectation**: this is a *one-session* story. When everyone knows that's the
goal, the whole table works toward it.

---

## Step 1 — The Inciting Quest

A one-shot starts *after* the party has accepted the quest. Because you already gave them the quest in
Step 0, the game can open right at the location where it takes place — e.g. as they leave the tavern, in
front of the abandoned manor, or in a cart on the way there (handy if there's a travel encounter).

**Decide:** what is the quest?

*Examples:* Find the Amulet · Defeat the Demon · Win the Race · Escort the Duke · Survive the Night ·

## Step 2 — The Two Paths

To give players real choice, design **two paths**:

- **Path of Least Resistance** — the most obvious route, leading directly to the quest in the least time.
  *(Locket quest: the party is told the locket is in the manor's basement, so the direct path is to go
  straight to the basement.)*
- **Path Least Traveled On** — the meandering route. It leads to extra encounters and it may not be
  obvious how it helps complete the quest. *(Locket quest: the party learns the ghost was a maid who
  lived on the top floor, so this path is to visit the maid's room.)* Players who take it find **the Key**.

*Examples of Paths Least Traveled On:* Ask the Smith Who Made the Amulet · Find Out Who Summoned the
Demon · Spy on the Competition · Look Into the Duke · Find a New Place to Camp · Find the Murder Weapon ·
Study the Labyrinth's Legends.

## Step 3 — The Key

The **Key** is something that *unlocks additional choices*. It can be a literal object or a piece of
information that **recontextualizes the quest**. It sits at the end of the Path Least Traveled On.

*(Locket quest: a diary revealing the quest-giver murdered the ghost; or a clue that the locket is cursed
and harms anyone who touches it; or a way to perform the exorcism differently.)*

Choosing whether to take the direct path or chase the Key **is itself a meaningful choice**.

*Examples of Keys:* The Amulet Is Cursed · The Demon Is Nice · The Race Is Rigged · The Duke Is Dead ·
The Players Cannot Survive · There Is More Than One Killer · The Labyrinth Has Two Levels.

## Step 4 — The Encounters

Encounters are the bulk of the one-shot and where **modularity** matters. Write most encounters so they
are **easy to cut** if you're running short. Two ways to do that:

- Write encounters so they **don't depend on one another**, or
- Provide a **bypass** that still hands the party whatever they need to move on.

Put the **bulk of encounters on the Path of Least Resistance**; the Path Least Traveled On needs **at
least one** extra encounter. Recommended starting mix for a standard one-shot:

| # | Encounter type |
|---|----------------|
| 2 | Combat encounters |
| 1 | Roleplay encounter |
| 1 | Exploration challenge or puzzle |

Write more if you like — but **most (ideally all) should be easily skippable**. Modularity is the goal.

*Examples:* A Safe Locked by a Puzzle · A Battle with a Diabolist · A Racing Skill Challenge · An Ambush ·
A Chase Scene · An Interrogation · A Trapped Battlefield.

## Step 5 — The Showdown

The **climax**, and the **only non-modular encounter** in the method. Usually a boss battle against the
last obstacle, but it can be anything — a tense roleplay scene, a timed puzzle, etc.

Depending on the **Key**, the circumstances around the showdown may change; you can even prep alternate
showdowns for different player choices. To save prep, **keep the mechanics the same and change the
flavor**.

*Examples:* A Bidding War for the Amulet · A Bombastic Battle Against a Demon · A Fight Atop a Moving
Vehicle · Waves of Enemies · A Trial to Unmask the Killer · A Mad Dash Out of a Crumbling Structure.

## Step 6 — The Endings *(prep two)*

Because choice is in the method's DNA, prepare **two endings**:

- **Ending A — "Adequate."** Requires *no* Key; happens if players go straight down the Path of Least
  Resistance. It's rare in practice (players usually explore), but it must exist so the choice is real.
  *They complete the quest the obvious way, and the one-shot ends.*
- **Ending B — "Ballsy."** Requires the **Key**, and should feel clearly *dependent* on having it, so
  players feel their choices mattered. *(Locket quest: if the Key revealed the quest-giver was the
  murderer, Ending B might be the party teaming up with the ghost to take the quest-giver down.)*

You can add extra choices during the ending without worrying about long-term ramifications — the
one-shot is wrapping up anyway.

*Examples of Ending Bs:* Destroy the Amulet · Befriend the Demon · Forfeit the Race · Kill the Duke ·
Escape the Night · Help the Killer Escape · Delve into the New Labyrinth.

## Step 7 — The Hook

The **Hook** is an implied continuation — a fun way to end that suggests these characters go on to
further adventures, and another way to make players feel their choices mattered. Make each hook **highly
dependent on the choices they made**. Hooks are also how many one-shots blossom into full campaigns.

*(Locket quest: if the party sided with the ghost, the hook could be her asking them to escort her to a
medium who can help her cross over. If they sided with the quest-giver, he might recommend them as
exorcists to a boarding school plagued by a tenacious ghost.)*

*Examples:* Create a New Amulet · Go to Hell · Qualify for the World Race · Find the Duke's Widow ·
Organize a Survival Game · Inform the Victim's Family · Explore the Other Three Labyrinths.
